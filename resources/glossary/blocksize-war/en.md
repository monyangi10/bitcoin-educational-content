---
term: BLOCKSIZE WAR
---

Refers to a period of intense debate and conflict within the Bitcoin community between 2015 and 2017. This conflict centered on whether the block size, which had been limited to 1 megabyte since 2010 by Satoshi Nakamoto, should be increased to allow Bitcoin to process more transactions per block, thereby improving scalability.

Proponents of increasing the block size (known as "*big blockers*"), led by early developers such as Jeff Garzik, Gavin Andresen, and Mike Hearn, influential figures in the ecosystem like Roger Ver, as well as a significant portion of the major mining companies, argued that this increase was necessary to reduce transaction fees and enhance Bitcoin's scalability. They believed that this change could be made without compromising the system's security. 
On the other hand, opponents of the block size increase (referred to as "*small blockers*"), mainly Bitcoin Core developers (Pieter Wuille, Peter Todd, Gregory Maxwell, Wladimir van der Laan, Luke Dashjr...) and a part of the technical community, argued that larger blocks would increase node operating loads, leading to centralization. Instead, they promoted second-layer scalability solutions, such as the Lightning Network, which could handle more transactions without altering Bitcoin’s base protocol.

While debate over Bitcoin scalability had existed since at least 2010, with an early attempt to raise the block size via [a patch proposed by Jeff Garzik](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=1347.0), the conflict peaked during the Blocksize War (2015-2017), featuring multiple fork attempts (Bitcoin XT, Bitcoin Classic, Bitcoin Unlimited, SegWit, SegWit2X, Bitcoin Cash), aggressive propaganda campaigns, and even personal attacks.

The SegWit2X hard fork was abandoned on November 8, 2017, following overwhelming community opposition ("NO2X"), which is often seen as the conclusion of the Blocksize War. While the casus belli is sometimes considered to be the update 0.11A of the Bitcoin XT client, which implemented BIP101 on August 15, 2015, it could also be argued that the Blocksize War ended on August 24, 2017, the date on which the soft fork SegWit was activated following the campaign in favor of the "UASF".
