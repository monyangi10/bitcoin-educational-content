---
term: PROOF OF RESERVATIONS
---

Mechanism used by bitcoin exchange platforms to demonstrate that they actually hold their customers' assets. The aim is to prove the company's solvency by verifying that its bitcoins are equivalent to or greater than the user balances they are supposed to hold.

Platforms often use Merkle trees that include all their users' balances. Each user can then verify that his or her balance is part of the total, without revealing information about other accounts. At the same time, the platform publicly signs the addresses that secure the bitcoins to prove that they have control over them.

However, these methods have significant limitations, as proof of possession of funds does not necessarily prove the company's solvency. Exchange could, for example, raise debts in secret by taking out Lombard loans secured by the same bitcoins used in the proof of reserve. It could also show a balance in borrowed bitcoins, while remaining insolvent. The problem with the reserve proof is that it focuses solely on verifying the platform's assets, without taking liabilities into account.

Traditionally on Bitcoin, to celebrate the anniversary of the publication of the Genesis block, every January 3 represents "Proof-of-Keys Day". Bitcoin holders are invited to withdraw their funds from the exchanges and store them in self-custody wallets, in order to test the solvency of these platforms.

But in the end, if you really want to protect yourself against the risk of exchange platform bankruptcy, the only solution is to keep your bitcoins in self-custody.
