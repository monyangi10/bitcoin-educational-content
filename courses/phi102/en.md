# Journey to freedom


This course explores liberty as a social project, drawing on the rich French liberal tradition of the 18th and 19th centuries. Through the writings of thinkers such as Frédéric Bastiat, Gustave de Molinari, Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, Benjamin Constant, and Charles Dunoyer, you will discover how liberty is not an abstract utopia, but deeply rooted in the facts of human existence.


Ready to discover why freedom is not only a moral ideal, but also the most effective solution to the challenges facing our societies? Let's begin this intellectual journey to the heart of French liberal thought.

# Foundations of freedom

<partId>466452a9-508e-5bae-bc6a-9213814cf1e5</partId>


## The human facts behind freedom and property

<chapterId>8eb03d97-ff42-51a1-bd42-9271d3213ac4</chapterId>




### Introduction to the French liberal tradition


The French liberal tradition, developed in the 18th and 19th centuries, constitutes a major part of the French intellectual heritage that the Coppet Institute is striving to rehabilitate. This intellectual tradition deserves attention for three reasons: the resurgence of ideas of liberty in contemporary debate, its deep roots in French-speaking culture, and its universalist perspective, which regards man as a human being rather than as a citizen of a particular time or country. The influence of thinkers like Frédéric Bastiat extends far beyond France's borders, and remains highly relevant today.


### Beyond theory-practice opposition and the coherence of freedom


Public debate artificially opposes theorists, dismissed as disconnected from reality, against practitioners, valued for their concrete knowledge. This dichotomy doesn't stand up to scrutiny: every one of our everyday actions, such as climbing stairs or picking up an object, is based on implicit theories such as gravity. Practitioners themselves constantly apply theoretical frameworks, often unconsciously. The real question is not to choose between theory and practice, but to distinguish good theories, based on observable facts, from bad ones, built on fallacies.


A problematic distinction has also been drawn between freedom, accepted as a fundamental value, and liberalism, often rejected as too abstract. This separation leads to the breaking of freedom into good and bad categories: political freedom is valued, while economic freedom is distrusted. Yet the principles underpinning religious tolerance and universal suffrage — in other words, the individual's capacity for choice - should logically lead to the recognition of economic freedom too. This lack of organisation reduces freedom to the simple faculty of periodically choosing a political master. Moreover, collective choice presents structural limitations: temporary and spaced out over time, they eliminate many individual choices that would enable constant adaptation to circumstances, direct responsibility and respect for the diversity of preferences.


### Ideologists and the factual foundations of freedom


Ideologists such as Destutt de Tracy, Cabanis, and Volney, active during the Revolution and the Empire, sought to anchor the theory of liberty in the observation of facts. Contrary to the image of the disconnected theorist, these thinkers were also practitioners: Cabanis was a physician, Destutt de Tracy was an agronomist. Napoleon gave them this pejorative name, but admitted on St. Helena that he himself did not know the purpose of his conquests. Ironically, it was the Ideologists who possessed a coherent understanding of human action, while the so-called practitioner acted without clear direction.


The first fundamental fact they identify is that the human being constitutes a body necessarily occupying a physical space. When someone sleeps in a bed, they occupy exclusively - even in a collectivist society, the bed belongs to them de facto while they are using it. Ownership thus emerges not as an arbitrary social construct, but as a direct consequence of our bodily existence. More fundamentally, appropriation appears to be required by nature: to eat an apple is necessarily to appropriate it; to breathe requires appropriation of the air. Ownership is therefore a reality rooted in our physical existence, not a convention that can be changed at will.


### The finitude of the world and the individuality of perceptions


The finite nature of resources inevitably gives rise to competition. Apples, for example, must be appropriated precisely because they are not infinite in number. While we can critique competition, we cannot eliminate it entirely. Collectivist systems do not remove it either; they simply redirect it into political relations, queues, or administrative privileges, often at the expense of efficiency and justice. The more pertinent question is not whether competition will exist, but what rules should govern it.


Furthermore, sensations are inherently individual. One cannot experience hunger through another person’s eyes, nor can one truly see through someone else’s perspective. This individuality carries significant implications for social organization: decisions based on direct personal perceptions contain information that collective decisions cannot replicate. For instance, a collective decision regarding food must aggregate the perceptions of multiple individuals, each with different needs in terms of timing and intensity. As a result, its outcomes will inevitably be less tailored to each person’s actual situation. These observations demonstrate that freedom is not merely one option among many, but a necessity arising from the very nature of human beings and the world they inhabit



## Freedom of expression, freedom of the press and free education

<chapterId>e19775a3-6b1d-50ed-ba6b-979f93a78b4b</chapterId>




### The natural foundations of freedom of thought


Freedom is based on concrete human realities rather than abstract constructs. The human being is not just a body acting in the material world: they are first and foremost a mind capable of feeling, conceiving and sharing ideas. Charles Comte, in his course on natural law (circa 1830), asserts that an individual devoid of cognitive abilities would scarcely be recognized as human. Social relationships are formed precisely because individuals can exchange thoughts and feelings.


This ability to think is accompanied by an intrinsic pleasure that naturally encourages its development and sharing. The story of Father Yves-Marie André illustrates this reality: when his superiors forbade him to carry out philosophical research, he suffered deeply. For many people, developing and sharing their thoughts is a fundamental source of satisfaction. Thought is a manifestation of individual will and an extension of our individuality: we feel, conceive projects and naturally want to communicate them.


### The inviolability of conscience and intellectual property


Thought is a logical consequence of the will, and its expression is merely its material manifestation. Since we possess free will, no one can prevent us from thinking. There is, therefore a natural postulate in favor of free expression: to hinder this freedom would break the organic link between sensation, will, thought and expression. When a person writes down their thoughts, it is always their personal property, directly tied to self-ownership.


This inviolability of conscience is historically demonstrated by the ineffectiveness of burning at the stake and intellectual repression. While the physical existence of the bearer of an idea can be suppressed, the act of thinking itself cannot be prevented. No authority has ever had power over thought as such, only over its material expression. We are free to change our ideas, pursue them, construct concepts or abandon them. Benjamin Constant, in his "Principes de politique" (1815), makes this point forcefully: "Error or truth, man's thought is his most sacred property." To break this freedom in the name of a higher reason, whatever it may be, seems fundamentally unjust.


### Human perfectibility and the debate of ideas


Freedom of expression is also justified by its contribution to human progress. Condorcet, in his Esquisse d'un tableau historique des progrès de l'esprit humain, develops the idea that humanity is continually advancing, thereby challenging the doctrine of the original fall. However, this perfectibility depends on certain conditions: new, daring, or revolutionary ideas must be freely communicated so that discoveries can be transmitted to subsequent generations.

Intellectual debate operates on a principle similar to economic competition: it allows the best ideas to prevail through confrontation. Everyone can assess whether a new conception aligns with their own observations and experiences. This dynamics serves as an essential mechanism of progress, enabling the continuous enhancement of humanity's intellectual heritage. Furthermore, the natural variety among human beings inevitably generates a diversity of ideas. Individuals with different dispositions develop distinct tastes, perceptions, and sensibilities. This diversity fully justifies the necessity of free debate, allowing the most well-founded ideas to reach their audience.

### Press freedom and free education


Freedom of the press plays a specific role in the functioning of democracy. Political actions have different effects on different people, and everyone must be able to express their perception of the consequences of a policy. This freedom enables debate on institutions, their constructive criticism and gradual reform. The Abbé de Saint-Pierre compared political institutions to clocks that need to be wound regularly.


French history illustrates the consequences of the absence of this freedom. Despite the influence of the Enlightenment, the absence of newspapers accessible to the people prevented the formation of an informed public opinion capable of supporting Turgot's reforms. In the end, these changes could only be brought about by the Revolution. The press acted as a "court of public opinion", a free and rapid mechanism for observing the effects of laws and pointing out their problematic aspects.

## Property and freedom of work

<chapterId>9b71cfae-425c-5255-a0f2-7a5b6805e284</chapterId>





### Self-ownership as the primary foundation


Self-ownership is the foundation of all thinking about economic freedom. Without recognition of the individual as self-owner, it is impossible to have legitimate ownership of material goods.


This property is articulated around two fundamental cycles. The first goes from sensation to judgment, then to opinion: our sensory perceptions are personal and individual, our judgments remain free, and their expression leads to freedom of the press, education and religious tolerance. The second cycle links observation, reasoning and adaptation: the individual observes, establishes links between cause and effect, then acts according to a personal plan of conduct.


Jules Simon demonstrated that complex economic notions such as capital or freedom of labor are based on simple human facts: free thought and ownership of one's own thoughts.


### Ownership of things and land


The ownership of things is based on the need to acquire. Human existence can only be sustained through the appropriation and production of goods. Vital necessities guide production, which requires the reasoning of self-ownership.


Appropriation necessarily precedes consumption: to consume an apple, you must first appropriate it. This appropriation is individual in nature, and it is precisely because we appropriate goods ourselves that we feel their effects personally.


Land ownership is an extension of this logic, and is based on work. The image of the sweaty land-clearer illustrates this reality: virgin land remains unsuitable for satisfying human needs. To make it productive, it must be cleared, cultivated and maintained. Possession is truly work, an observation that extends even to capital: preserving and increasing capital requires resistance to the temptations of immediate consumption and the ability to direct investments wisely.


### The universality of ownership


Ownership can be observed in all ages and societies. Phases of human development correspond to different forms of property, depending on the conditions of production, but the fundamental principle remains the same.


This notion can even be observed among animals, who share spaces and territories that they defend against intruders. Even in the Stone Age, caves and resources were regarded as private property, although often treated as group owned property. This collective tribal ownership should not be confused with common ownership by all humanity: tribes excluded other human groups.


This collective form of hunting can be explained by production conditions. Hunting could not be carried out in restricted areas, because animals moved over vast territories. Ownership existed, but adapted to the technical constraints of the time.


### Freedom to work, self-interest and free trade


Freedom to work is rooted in real, individual need. It is I who feel my own hunger, it is I who experience a particular suffering. This individuality is the basis of freedom to work: as the need is personal, one must be free to address it in the way they see fit.


A second foundation is the variety of dispositions, tastes and strengths between individuals. Faced with similar needs, individuals feel them differently. Each must find the resources to draw from his own body the utilities that will meet his specific needs. When a regulatory state intervenes, it breaks this natural chain by imposing a uniform course of action without taking individual dispositions into account.


Self-interest is a fundamental motive for human action. Pierre de Boisguilbert identified it as early as the beginning of the eighteenth century: all commerce is governed solely by the interests of entrepreneurs, who are never thinking of rendering service but of satisfying their own needs. This reciprocal utility is what makes the world harmonious.


Free trade is the logical extension of these principles. If I am an owner by virtue of my work, what authority can prevent me from exchanging my good for another that pleases me more? To legitimately own some good and not have the freedom to trade it is in contradiction with basic human realities.


Taken together, these observations do not constitute an abstract theory, but a rigorous appreciation of the facts of human nature, developed by the liberal economists of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.




## Non-aggression, respect for property and peace

<chapterId>e8519889-1178-521e-9de7-b32f6177d92b</chapterId>




### The physiological basis of the non-aggression principle


The principle of non-aggression has its roots in universal biological facts. Pain is the mechanism through which violations of our freedom and bodily integrity become perceptible to us. Without this phenomenon, transmitted by the nervous system, it would be impossible to perceive violations of our physical integrity. This physiological reality establishes a natural limit to everyone's freedom: any action that causes pain to others constitutes an infringement of their self-ownership.


A second fact completes this understanding: fear and the inhibition of action it produces. Gustave de Molinari developed this analysis: when others infringe our rights or pose a threat, we are compelled to reconsider and adjust our plans. This psychological constraint prevents us from acting as we would have done without the threat, diminishing our effective freedom.


However, not all nuisances can be treated equally by the law. The example of the baker illustrates this distinction: a new competitor causes an economic nuisance, but this does not constitute a legally actionable aggression. Historical developments show a gradual expansion of the nuisances that the law no longer considers punishable. In the Middle Ages, competition and technical progress were often condemned because their effects were concentrated in restricted communities. The emancipation of these restrictions accompanied the development of a freer society.


### Social harmony and peace through exchange


Frédéric Bastiat and Gustave de Molinari summed up their doctrine as "liberty and peace". This harmony is rooted in the relationship between customers and suppliers. The customer needs his supplier to obtain the desired goods, while the supplier needs his customer to be prosperous. A rational supplier prefers wealthy customers capable of acquiring his products. This logic applies at every level, from the family to international trade, creating mutually beneficial relationships that naturally engender harmony.


The Abbé de Saint-Pierre underlines a fundamental fact: security is a universal need, because no individual is strong enough to protect themselves alone. Even the strongest person was not strong in childhood, nor will he be in old age. Even at the peak of strength, individuals experience moments of vulnerability— such as sleep, illness, and weakening. This vulnerability creates a universal need for collective security, satisfied by the establishment of the rule of law.


The evolution of modern production reinforces this tendency towards peace. Hunting and fishing are violent forms of production, based on destruction rather than creation. Agriculture represents an advance towards peaceful production, embodying harmony rather than predatory activities.


### The foundations of political freedom


Political freedom is grounded in fundamental facts about human nature. The principle of decentralization is rooted in human variety: human beings have different needs, preferences, and circumstances. This diversity is above all individual. Logically, decentralization should extend to individual autonomy. This reasoning, defended by Benjamin Constant, highlights the fundamental impossibility of central planning: one cannot organize society in a centralized manner while ignoring this irreducible diversity.


Universal suffrage is based on the fact that pleasures and pains are individual experiences: everyone feels the effects of laws directly. People may perceive the same situation differently and form judgments based on their own experiences. This reality justifies involving everyone in the democratic process.


### The foundations of civil liberty


Civil liberty is likewise grounded in fundamental truths. Freedom of expression and religious tolerance arise from the reality that judgments stem from individual personality and self-ownership. Because personalities differ, judgments will — and must — differ as well. This diversity makes possible the exchange of ideas, which is the driving force behind human improvement. Rather than an obstacle, this plurality of viewpoints is a resource to be cultivated.


The question of marriage illustrates these principles: the choice of spouse must be free, because there are a thousand shades of beauty and a thousand ways of being happy. Aesthetic and emotional perceptions are profoundly individual. These simple facts support the essential elements of civil liberty, showing that liberalism is rooted in the concrete reality of human experience, rather than in abstract ideological constructions.



## The factual contradictions of constraint systems

<chapterId>c13f7395-6734-5c64-b86c-af7922ffbcb0</chapterId>




### The contradictions surrounding property


Proudhon's formula "property is theft" illustrates the internal contradictions of systems of constraint. Bastiat demonstrated its logical incoherence: if property is theft, then theft would be property, which is equivalent to claiming that what is legitimate is illegitimate. His analysis establishes that property flows naturally from work, self-ownership, and human faculties, present in all societies throughout history.


Non-freedom systems postulate an original spoliation of land by force, followed by enjoyment without work. This vision contains two major factual errors. Firstly, it assumes an automatic ease to enrichment, ignoring the constant risks, back-breaking work and difficulties faced by entrepreneurs. Secondly, if this original despoilment were true, we would expect to see the descendants of these land-grabbers among the wealthiest families today. However, reality shows that exchanges are legitimately made through work and the exchange of value for value. Today's properties have been validated by work and voluntary exchange. Moreover, production conditions are constantly evolving: lands that were once valuable can lose their worth, while previously neglected areas can gain value, as illustrated by rare earths earths illustrates.


### The contradictions of human nature


Uniform laws and administrative centralization are in direct contradiction with humanity's natural diversity. These systems impose uniform ways of working and enjoying property, whereas property should confer the freedom to do with it as one wishes. Under these regimes, the owner becomes a mere usufructuary, with true ownership belonging to the state, reminiscent of the Ancien Régime when the king was the ultimate owner of all land.


Another major contradiction is the weakening of self-interest. Every human being is born with a distinct personality that manifests itself throughout their life. Freedom allows self-interest to find its satisfactions voluntarily and peacefully, at its own pace and according to its own particular needs. Non-freedom imposes standardized satisfactions that do not correspond to people's actual needs.


The rejection of parental love in education represents a particularly serious contradiction. Unfree education replaces these natural feelings with state administrative guardianship. The state, devoid of feelings, cannot appreciate the unique personality of each child, unlike parents, who possess irreplaceable intimate knowledge and emotional investment.


### Thought control and its contradictions


Attempts to control thought are manifested through the subsidized press, monopolistic national education, limitations on religious freedom and regulated democracy. These measures are attacks on the very nature of human thought. Thought is formed within, belongs intimately to each individual and is the purest expression of self-ownership. Systems of coercion attempt to break the natural link between ideas and their peaceful expression, depriving society of the process of discovering and improving the truth that emerges from free debate.

This approach reveals a profound misunderstanding of how ideas are formed and propagated. Thought cannot be controlled from the outside without destroying the ability to think creatively and critically. Systems that impose intellectual orthodoxy end up stifling innovation and intellectual progress essential to human development


### Economic contradictions


Competition is a fundamental fact of life, made necessary by the finiteness of resources. Systems of unfreedom wage an impossible battle against it, without understanding that it cannot be suppressed, only transformed. It exists in two forms: violent competition and peaceful competition through exchange. Political competition represents a disguised pursuit of violence, where a group acquires resources by legal force. These systems replace peaceful competition with violent competition, creating unjust relationships.


The abolition of the law of supply and demand reveals a fundamental misunderstanding. This law is simply the expression of the effects of freedom on human actions: when a price rises, individuals naturally reduce their demand. This behavior is universal and cannot be suppressed by decree.


On the subject of collective ownership, Paul Leroy-Beaulieu explained that the enjoyment of something owned in common is extremely limited. Each citizen, theoretically the owner of an infinitesimal fraction of a public forest, has no sense of ownership. Collectivization leads to the loss of the "master's eye", that special attention and incentive to improvement that only the true owner possesses. The inevitable result is neglect and sub-optimal use of resources.


Finally, the abolition of interest on money and the regulation of profits are attacks on voluntary exchange mechanisms. Interest simply represents the price of transmission between the giver and the taker of capital, reflecting the value of time and risk for the lender.


# History of freedom

<partId>367e5e75-44e1-5fab-803f-744cf5ddc85c</partId>


## Prehistoric man and primitive communism

<chapterId>ed3a71e6-6e1b-504a-8c53-fe21b891ad27</chapterId>




### Historical debates on the origin of freedom


Freedom is not an immutable natural state, but a historical construct that develops progressively over the centuries. This thesis, defended by 19th-century liberal thinkers, is in direct opposition to the myth of a past golden age in which humanity enjoyed original freedom.


The debate between Germaine de Staël and Benjamin Constant illustrates this fundamental opposition. Staël, in his critique of Napoleon, argued that freedom was ancient and despotism modern. Constant refuted this view, demonstrating that ancient freedom was neither real nor complete. Liberal thinkers have extended this analysis, establishing that each century brings technical and economic improvements that make freedom more practicable. Understanding this evolution helps us to understand why freedom is more feasible today than it was in antiquity.


The myths of Rousseau's bon sauvage and the primitive social contract have been systematically criticized by liberals. The idea that autonomy and full human power were found in the past does not stand up to examination of the actual conditions of existence in primitive societies. As for the social contract, the elements needed to establish it were lacking at the supposed time of its creation: developed language, the very conception of society beyond the family. Society is not a voluntary creation born of conscious agreement; it exists from the outset as a natural condition of human existence.


### The material constraints of primitive societies


In prehistoric and ancient societies, self-ownership, the foundation of modern freedom, was virtually non-existent. Children belonged to their fathers, women to their husbands or fathers, and could be sold or exchanged as material goods. Abbé de Saint-Pierre explains this situation by the extreme weakness of productive power, limited to hunting, fishing and gathering. Without adult male support, survival was impossible. A woman who wanted to "feel freedom" by running away from her camp would have faced certain death, for lack of independent productive capacity.


The individual was entirely subject to the tribe, with a set of rules governing all his or her actions, including the most personal. The private sphere we define today as a space of freedom did not exist. These societies practiced the systematic sacrifice of individuals considered incapable or weak: abortion and the sacrifice of widows and the elderly were regular practices, often sanctioned by laws and religions. These practices can be explained by the extremely difficult material circumstances: ungrateful and unproductive nature, constant climatic and human dangers, complete submission to the vagaries of nature.


Private ownership of land was also impossible to establish. Productive activities of the time (hunting, fishing, gathering) required common ownership. Buffalo hunting, for example, could not be practised in an enclosed area of just a few hectares: people had to live nomadically over vast expanses of land. Communal ownership was not the result of an ideological preference, but a rational adaptation to material constraints.


### The impossibility of freedom to act and think


Charles Dunoyer has shown that freedom is a progressive construct: we are freer in industrial societies than in feudal societies, than under ancient slavery, than in prehistoric times. What were we free of in primitive times? Nothing, because human existence was subject to all kinds of constraints.


Lack of security was a major obstacle to freedom of action. Why take action when its fruits are constantly compromised by danger? The omnipotence of custom also prevented any real freedom: in the face of a lack of security, it was thought better to impose a direction on man than to leave him with his feeble lights in the face of overly difficult circumstances.


The freedom to think could hardly be exercised either. Intelligence is developed daily through education and observation. But how could intelligence be mobilized when production depended mainly on brute force? Productive activities relied on the use of physical force to obtain the wealth necessary for survival. Technical innovations, of which the steam engine is an emblematic example, enabled freedom of thought to become effective. The comparison between a porter and a locomotive driver illustrates this evolution: these two professions do not require the same human dispositions, the latter allowing a much more complete development of intellectual capacities.


### Freedom as a progressive conquest


Historical analysis shows that freedom is not a lost natural state to be regained, but a progressive conquest made possible by the evolution of material, technical and social conditions. The new circumstances that emerge with economic development create the necessary conditions for the effective exercise of freedom. In our modern societies, freedom is imposed not by ideology, but because material conditions finally allow it to be exercised effectively. This historical perspective sheds light on why freedom is an essential project for contemporary societies: it represents the culmination of a secular evolution that technical and economic transformations now make not only possible, but necessary.




## The ancient raison d'être of slavery, serfdom and political authority

<chapterId>29fc841c-eb21-522d-818c-9b50c6558396</chapterId>




### Slavery: a response to primitive economic constraints


Slavery developed universally in ancient societies, as it responded to the economic constraints of the time. Production depended almost exclusively on physical strength, whether for hunting, fighting or conquest. Productive yields remained low and uncertain, transporting foodstuffs represented a major challenge, and trade remained extremely limited. In this context, slavery was an institution consistent with the productive logic of societies where force was the principal means of acquiring resources.


Beyond its economic role, slavery fulfilled a social insurance function in a world marked by permanent insecurity. For some workers, this status enabled them to contribute to production without risking their lives on a daily basis in dangerous activities. As Aristotle observed, slavery existed because there were no machines. If production could have been mechanized, slaves would have been dispensable.


### The gradual obsolescence of slavery


Transformations in production have revealed the fundamental limits of the slave system. When economic activity became more intellectual, requiring creativity and innovation, slavery proved profoundly inadequate. A slave can perform physical tasks under duress, but he cannot invent or apply his mental vigor productively. At the same time, improved security conditions have enabled individuals to develop their capacity for autonomy and assume the risks of an independent existence.


These transformative forces led to gradual abolition and mutation into serfdom, and then into other, less restrictive forms of domination. This evolution was particularly evident in societies affected by technical progress, where new circumstances provided concrete support for the expansion of freedom.


### The liberal critique of modern slavery


The maintenance of slavery in America in the 18th and 19th centuries was a major historical paradox. French liberal thinkers, such as Gustave de Beaumont, recognized that slavery may have had some justification in antiquity, but denounced contemporary slavery as totally unjustifiable and artificially maintained by political authority.


They identified two pernicious effects of the system. Firstly, the dumbing-down of the slaves themselves: unable to call upon their intellectual capacities, the system deprived them of any possibility of personal development. Secondly, the moral degradation of the owners, who developed a negative view of work and resorted only to force. The habit of dominating others engendered profoundly illiberal conceptions, incompatible with the principles of equality that should underpin modern societies.


### Political subjection and the historical dynamic towards freedom


Political subjection had its roots in the same circumstances as slavery: the imperative need for security in a hostile environment. The absence of a leader in vital activities such as hunting or fighting could mean starvation or death for the entire group. This need for leadership gave rise to considerable political powers covering all aspects of social life. Narrow markets and poor communications reinforced this dependence, leaving individuals with no viable alternatives.


Examination of the historical reasons for unfreedom reveals a fundamental trend: technical transformations have progressively altered the conditions of existence, making freedom not only possible but necessary. The institutions that once limited freedom are losing their raison d'être as progress creates new possibilities for autonomy. Freedom thus appears not as an abstract ideal, but as a concrete reality whose realization depends on the material and social conditions of each era.


## Emancipating technical and intellectual progress

<chapterId>a9cf7eb2-4639-5b8a-8fd6-2ad0a9e7cff2</chapterId>




### From destruction to production


The first human societies did not produce new wealth, but instead extracted resources from their environment by hunting, fishing and gathering. This economy of destruction imposed absolute dependence on the natural renewal of resources, limiting the size of groups and preventing the emergence of complex societies.


Contrary to Rousseau's image of the first landowner as a despoiler, historical research shows that collective ownership already existed on a tribal scale, with regular conflicts between groups. Whoever enclosed a plot of land actually transformed the mode of production: by mastering breeding and agriculture, he created a surplus, generating more resources than he consumed. This productive revolution opened up new possibilities, enabling population growth and the financing of collective protection functions.


### Safety, production and social development


Dupont de Nemours observed in 1765 that a crop without a surplus can only support scattered families, incapable of forming a nation or financing a protective authority. The resources generated by productive agriculture make it possible to introduce greater security into human relations, favoring peaceful, contractual exchanges over warlike power relations.


The relationship between security and economic progress presents a paradox: security is both a prerequisite and a consequence of development. Without it, it is impossible to innovate or invest in the long term; but only a production surplus can finance the institutions that guarantee social order. This virtuous cycle, which is difficult to initiate, explains the historically slow pace of social transformation. Once set in motion, it tends to reinforce itself progressively.


### Technical progress and the transformation of social relations


Capitalism refers to all the technical advances that gradually replaced brute force with the machine as the central element of production. This evolution puts an end to domination based on physical power, and favours peaceful relations of exchange. When productivity depends more on intelligence than muscle power, traditional relationships of domination lose their justification.


This transformation particularly affected the status of women. In hunting societies, women's position remained subordinate, as their economic contribution was deemed secondary. Sophisticated machines and tools, requiring less strength and more spirit, enabled the emergence of self-ownership for all, regardless of physical strength. At the same time, the development of transport infrastructures expanded markets, subjecting economic players to competition and encouraging them to operate by contract rather than by constraint.


### Material conditions and personal freedom


These transformations have also altered the value placed on innovation. In ancient societies, the innovator was viewed with suspicion because he created uncertainty. New material conditions have enabled new ideas to flourish: whereas in the Middle Ages innovators were persecuted, they have become the heroes of modern societies.


Examination of the different stages of development reveals a deep correlation between material conditions and the potential for individual freedom. Early societies, characterized by low production and extreme sensitivity to natural hazards, offered only limited scope for personal autonomy. As societies developed their technical capacities and created surpluses, they became more independent of natural constraints. Freedom is therefore not an abstract philosophical aspiration, but depends fundamentally on the material conditions that enable human beings to free themselves from the most pressing necessities.



## History at a glance

<chapterId>3fffa63c-58bb-5c97-a2f5-fec90c05f182</chapterId>




### Obstacles to freedom in primitive societies


In ancient societies, several structural factors prevented the establishment of freedom and private property. Production was characterized by extreme precariousness, with results that were insufficient and subject to many hazards. The very conditions of production, notably hunting, required vast territories incompatible with small private property. This material reality imposed forms of collective organization in which individual freedom remained more an aspiration than a concrete reality.


The birth of agriculture represented a major turning point. It introduced peaceful production and the accumulation of surpluses, creating the conditions for the development of commercial exchanges. At the same time, contractual relations emerged, introducing interactions based on mutual agreement rather than force. These material changes form the foundation on which social structures promoting individual freedom can be built.


### A new approach to history: the gradual conquest of freedom


Historical analysis gains in relevance when it focuses on the gradual conquest of freedom rather than on the simple succession of political powers. This perspective reveals how freedom gradually imposes itself and why, by showing the links between technical progress, economic transformations and the evolution of political ideas.


Ancient societies, Antiquity and the Middle Ages, were organized differently precisely because their production techniques differed radically. Political systems were not arbitrary, but were imposed according to material circumstances. Several major transitions mark this progression: the beginnings of agriculture, the transition from slavery to serfdom, the birth of industry, major inventions such as the steam engine, and the discovery of new continents, which opened up markets and created the conditions for competition.


### The Enlightenment and freedom of trade


The Enlightenment movement represents the emergence of new ideas that find their application because material conditions have changed. Physiocrats like Turgot, and philosophers like Condorcet, anticipated the increasing progress of humanity. This notion of perfectibility led them to conceive of institutions based on much greater freedom. The practical usefulness of freedom became apparent in the new economic conditions.


In contrast, in the Middle Ages, free competition could be considered impractical. The baker in a small town had a reserved market, creating a domination of producers over consumers. In the 18th century, the physiocrats observed the birth of world trade and demonstrated that these new circumstances called for free trade, enabling prices to be balanced and goods to be obtained at the lowest cost.


### Reforms versus revolutions: two paths to freedom


The French example of the 18th century illustrates the need to harmonize ideas with facts. The major problem was the impossibility of bringing new ideas into line with social realities without violent revolution. Gradual reforms, guided by informed public opinion, normally enable ideas to be brought into line with facts, and freedom to be extended peacefully.


Revolutions have significant drawbacks: they generate a lot of violence and tend to impose more change than public opinion is ready for. This inadequacy explains the frequent backtracking that follows revolutionary periods. The patient work of gradual reform is preferable, as it allows us to adopt greater freedom when conditions truly permit. Current circumstances are materially the most favorable in human history for freedom, property and individual autonomy to become the pillars of modern societies.


## The new circumstances of the 21st century

<chapterId>a1c0c590-d0f6-5a18-a949-67f7a568ca6d</chapterId>




### Obstacles to freedom in primitive societies


The historical study of freedom reveals a fascinating progression, transforming this concept from a distant ideal into a practicable reality. In ancient societies, several structural factors prevented the establishment of freedom and private property. Production was characterized by extreme precariousness, with results that were insufficient and subject to many hazards. Subsistence, especially hunting, required vast territories incompatible with private property. These technical and economic constraints explain why freedom remained a dream rather than a concrete reality: collective survival necessarily took precedence over individual autonomy.


### Agriculture and trade as factors of emancipation


The birth of agriculture represented a major historical turning point. This revolution introduced peaceful production, enabling the accumulation of surplus resources and the development of trade. At the same time, contractual relations emerged, introducing interactions based on mutual agreement rather than force. Several major transitions mark this progression: the transition from slavery to serfdom, the birth of small-scale industry, major inventions such as the steam engine, and the discovery of new continents. The development of transport infrastructures and international trade broke the isolation of local markets, stimulating competition and balancing out agricultural uncertainties between regions.


### The Enlightenment and freedom of trade


The Enlightenment was a particularly significant moment in this evolution. The thinkers of this period, notably physiocrats like Turgot and philosophers like Condorcet, did not theorize abstractly: they anticipated a growing progress of humanity based on human perfectibility. Their fundamental recognition is that freedom actually works in the new economic conditions. By contrast, in the Middle Ages, free competition might have seemed impractical: the baker in a small town had a reserved market, creating a domination of producers over consumers. In the 18th century, the Physiocrats observed the birth of world trade and demonstrated that these new circumstances called for free trade, enabling prices to be balanced and goods to be obtained at the lowest cost.


### Reforms versus revolutions


The French example of the 18th century illustrates the difficulty of matching new ideas to social realities. How can enlightened public opinion be created without a free press? Progressive reforms, guided by informed opinion, normally allow ideas to be peacefully reconciled with facts. Revolutions, on the other hand, have major drawbacks: they generate a great deal of violence and tend to impose more change than the population is ready for, which explains the numerous backtracking that follows them. The patient work of gradual reform is preferable, as it allows us to embrace freedom when conditions truly permit.


This reflection leads to an essential conclusion: today's circumstances are materially the most favorable to freedom ever seen in human history. The historical lesson is clear: freedom is not imposed by decree or violence, but emerges naturally when material, technical and intellectual conditions create the right environment for it to flourish.


# How freedom works

<partId>d8b75eca-d6c5-535c-a659-2a0320783baf</partId>


## The ability to govern oneself

<chapterId>aeca263f-cce0-5025-829e-4480b73394a6</chapterId>




### The prerequisite for freedom


For freedom to truly assert itself as a project for society and progress in history, it is essential to make good use of it. This fundamental requirement presupposes a prior apprenticeship, because freedom that is poorly exercised can backfire on the person who uses it. Two essential principles guide this learning process: do no harm to yourself, and do no harm to others. When an individual squanders his own strength or uses his freedom in an uninformed way, he risks destroying the very freedom he seeks to exercise. By the same token, those who attack the freedom of others wage war against humankind and society, which inevitably leads to a diminution of their own freedom through the intervention of judicial and police institutions.


In addition to these two protective principles, we must also seek to use freedom to maximum effect. This optimization of freedom is made possible by the technological and technical developments mentioned above, the fruit of a long historical process. The analysis of history should focus more on the gradual development of freedom and its apprenticeship than on the simple succession of political powers or territorial battles.


### The individual causes of unfreedom: harming oneself


Alcoholism is a particularly enlightening example of a use of freedom that ultimately harms the person exercising it. Although it initially represents a free act, it gradually diminishes the individual's capacity for freedom by placing him or her in a state of dependency. The immediate effects of excessive consumption are compounded by more distant effects in the form of loss of faculties, reduced willpower and diminished self-possession. The individual thus becomes less able to use his or her capacities, including mental ones.


More generally, intemperance and all actions or habits that deprave, irritate or stultify our faculties are obstacles to true freedom. The more these behaviours impair our capacities, the less free we are to make enlightened use of them, or any use at all. This dimension of the self-limitation of freedom by destructive choices deserves special attention in any reflection on the conditions of a free society.


### Individual causes of unfreedom: harming others


When an individual harms others by threatening, endangering or violating their rights, he or she is at war with humankind. The Abbé de Saint-Pierre, in his work on the project of perpetual peace, explains how security is born in human societies, and how it must progressively extend to the national and then the international level. The absence of security not only diminishes the freedom of others, but also that of the aggressor himself, since in organized societies, to be an enemy of the freedom of others is to suffer a severe loss of freedom.


Charles Dunoyer, in his 1825 work entitled "L'industrie et la morale dans leur rapport avec la liberté" (Industry and Morality in Relation to Freedom), followed by "De la liberté du travail" (On the Freedom of Labor) in 1845, analyzes the vices of primitive man that make him less free than modern societies can offer. Drawing on the accounts of travelers who had visited societies resembling those of the past, he describes the voracity of New Holland natives who pounce on their prey, the incontinence of the savage whose licentious morals become excessive as soon as his condition softens, and above all the improvidence that prevents any lasting construction. These testimonies contradict the representations inherited from Rousseau, and demonstrate that freedom is a construction, a becoming and an apprenticeship dependent on the moralization and industrialization of men.


### Historical progress towards a society of free men


Progress in self-government has enabled major advances since ancient and primitive times, leading from slavery to serfdom, and then to increasingly unregulated and limited freedom. Today, we are gradually arriving at a society of potentially free men, precisely because they have acquired the moral and intellectual capacities necessary to make enlightened use of their freedom. This historical evolution demonstrates that freedom is not a natural state, but the result of a civilizational process.


Having established these preconditions, it is now possible to examine the factual elements concerning freedom, in particular its effects and how it operates in practice. While freedom is a factual reality, and follows logically from historical analysis as the culmination of a great process of progress, we still need to understand how it works and why it works the way it does. This understanding is essential if we are to turn freedom into a coherent and feasible project for society.




## Production and contracts

<chapterId>0cb003fe-58ac-5fa5-89b1-97e558fc98ac</chapterId>




### Production as historical conquest and utility creation


Understanding economic freedom is a major issue in our reflection on freedom as a project for society. This economic dimension of freedom is often poorly understood, not because it is intrinsically complex, but because economic education remains insufficient, particularly in France. The mechanisms of production and exchange, though relatively simple, need to be clarified to understand how a liberal society is essentially based on contract, exchange and peaceful production.


Production represents a real conquest in the history of mankind. For a long time, human societies were content to gather foodstuffs without any particular input, through hunting, fishing and gathering, where man's productive activity remained minimal. To analyze production correctly, we need to understand it as the creation of new utility, as a service rendered to others or to ourselves. Producing does not mean creating matter, since man can neither create nor destroy an atom, in accordance with Lavoisier's principle. The retailer, for example, by transferring and making available to the consumer the foodstuffs he needs in an accessible and organized way, renders a genuine service. This transfer from one place to another constitutes genuine production, for which the consumer is prepared to pay.


### The fallacy of productive and unproductive workers


Adam Smith, among other thinkers, made a significant error in distinguishing between productive and unproductive workers. The unproductive category included lawyers, artists and doctors, on the grounds that their product would disappear immediately after delivery. This view is profoundly flawed. A teacher's lesson does not disappear; it constitutes an element of intellectual and immaterial production whose effects endure. Gustave de Molinari rightly pointed out that some books are good tools, illustrating that production extends far beyond mere material transformation. Work, generally speaking, is about creating new utilities and services. To be of service to others is what production really means.


### The conditions required for production


The conditions of production reveal a path that is both theoretical and historical. The first requirement is to make an appropriate choice of means and ends, which implies enlightened freedom and a sense of self-government. We need to own ourselves and be able to choose wisely between the various options available to us. This ability to choose requires freedom of thought and freedom of action. The second condition lies in the possible use of means, in other words, ownership. Without ownership of things, tools and one's own body, production becomes impossible. It all starts with self-ownership. Finally, the possibility of profitability is conditioned by security. Without security, I cannot set up a viable production project, as I risk being hindered by violence, threats, attacks or theft of my resources.


The direction of production obeys what is known as the natural law, according to which the production of everything organizes itself, in time, in place and in the most useful way. Economic agents seek their own interest, not a narrow, selfish one, but a broad, enlightened one. Motivated by the search for profit and security in their resources, they turn to those branches of production that yield profits. These profits signal the consumer's willingness to pay because these products make his life easier, enrich him and cover his needs. Prices and profits are thus signals that naturally guide production, without the need for governmental or regulatory organization.


### Exchange as a positive-sum game


The central element that explains the importance of contract and exchange lies in their nature as positive-sum games. I make a contract because it's in my interest to do so, otherwise I'd simply prefer not to contract. Similarly, in economic terms, if I make an exchange, it's because it's in my interest to do so. The Abbé de Saint-Pierre, in the early 18th century, recognized the importance of this notion particularly well. In his project for perpetual peace, he wrote that when a sale is made between merchants, the seller gains and so does the buyer, for without a reciprocal gain, neither would sell at such a price, nor would the other buy. Sometimes one of them makes a mistake, but usually, given their needs and interests, both gain from the exchange.


From this analysis stems a fundamental conclusion: increasing trade between the subjects of a nation and between nations contributes to their enrichment, while decreasing trade reduces their profit and income. This logic applies more broadly to contracts in all aspects of social life. The more contracts are made, the more advantageously the contract replaces the law in many areas. Freedom consists precisely in this choice to act in one direction or another with the properties at one's disposal, the first being one's own property, then that of legitimately acquired things, in order to exchange them in a mutually advantageous manner.



## The role of profits and prices

<chapterId>549e00c3-eece-5401-be62-b82c774af893</chapterId>




### Competition as the driving force behind historical progress


The history of mankind can be read as a gradual transformation of the ways in which individuals compete with one another. In the early days, competition was mainly exercised through war and violence, with the monopolization of goods and subsistence commodities obtained by hunting, fishing or gathering. The market remained extremely small, and production limited. As the world was made up of finite resources, some form of competition for them was inevitable. However, the real progress of civilization lay in replacing this violent competition with contract and voluntary exchange. This evolution marks a fundamental turning point in the organization of human societies, enabling individuals to satisfy their needs without recourse to force.


Unfortunately, interventionism and socialism represent a reverse step in human progress. These systems replace freedom with tutelage and centralized authority, substituting voluntary contract with a return to appropriation through coercion. The similarity between competition through violence and competition through politics is clearly seen in their respective effects. The distribution of wealth through violence or politics certainly redistributes existing resources, but does not increase them. On the other hand, competition through contract, based on the mutual benefit of the exchanging parties, distributes wealth fairly among the various contributors, while increasing overall production through the motivation of self-interest.


### Price as a fundamental signal of economic freedom


The law of supply and demand is the very workings of economic freedom, as Charles Coquelin explained in his article on competition published in 1853 in the Dictionnaire de l'économie politique. Without freedom, this law simply cannot operate. A citizen receiving ration coupons has no opportunity to reduce or increase his demand according to his preferences. Similarly, companies subject to political production decisions do not have the freedom to vary their supply according to circumstances. The law of supply and demand therefore presupposes freedom as an indispensable precondition for its operation.


In a free economy, price plays an essential signaling role. In a vast market with a large population, economic agents need a compass to guide them through production, knowing what to produce, when to produce it, where to produce it and how to produce it. Price provides precisely this crucial information by assigning a value to the various productive combinations. As it rises or falls, it guides the self-interest of individuals towards the actions that will enable them to achieve maximum satisfaction while meeting the real needs of society. Profits and interest rates are also signals of need or lack of need for the various factors of production.


### The programmed failure of centralized planning


Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, writing in 1884, anticipated with remarkable foresight the great developments of communism in the twentieth century. His analysis demonstrated that socialism, by rejecting profit and replacing the price mechanism with a centralized machine directing both production and consumption, was condemning itself to blind operation. Without the price signal, centralized management has no way of knowing real human needs, nor of perceiving the effects of changes in those needs or in the supply of production. This absence of signals makes the failure of centralized production and collectivist distribution inevitable.


The operation of freedom, on the other hand, enables timely production. High profits encourage new producers to participate in the manufacture of a given product, and this participation continues until enough producers have stepped in to satisfy the additional demand. The potential or anticipated price signals to all market agents what is to be produced, where and how. This spontaneous coordination also extends over space, with geographical distribution between different countries, and over time, since the price signal indicates when a technology becomes sufficiently valued to be profitable.


### The superiority of economic time over political time


In a system of freedom, we can rely on the self-interest and free will of individuals to seek out new opportunities as best they can. This pursuit of profit naturally involves failures and trial and error to distinguish real opportunities from those that turn out to be disappointing. Interventionism, on the other hand, uses political signals, organizing everything according to political time and electoral imperatives. In this way, we produce in a politically useful time, with strategic investments based on the idea that the State perceives latent needs in society.


The decisive advantage of economic freedom is that production is never too early or too late. When some entrepreneurs prematurely imagine that a demand exists for an innovation, the absence of profits dissuades them from pursuing it, and they are not followed. Conversely, when profits are within reach, ambitious entrepreneurs try the experiment and discover that consumer resources are available. They are then quickly followed by others, after a period of learning new production techniques. In this way, everything is done in economically useful time, based on real satisfactions demanded by consumers, rather than on idealized political satisfactions that don't exist in reality.


## Self-interest, innovation and progress

<chapterId>958075af-fb8f-50d9-9bf5-4d9882e03072</chapterId>




### Self-interest at the heart of economic liberalism


Economic liberalism is based on a concept of self-interest that is often misunderstood and caricatured. Alexis de Tocqueville, in his book De la démocratie en Amérique, observes that American society is founded on the pursuit of self-interest, with the law giving free rein to this natural aspiration. However, Tocqueville also observes that the only egoism of which individuals are truly capable consists in forgetting the greater society in order to think of themselves and their own. This observation reveals a fundamental truth: self-interest can never be reduced to strict, geometric egoism.


In reality, human beings are capable of a broad egoism, which begins with the family, then extends to a circle of acquaintances, a small business or an association. To speak of individuals entrenched in a narrow egoism does not correspond to the facts observed on a daily basis. Self-interest is rooted in human existence through the series of pleasures and sufferings that each person feels and measures according to his or her individual perception, personal construction and genetic heritage. The human person remains the sole judge of means and ends, which is the foundation of all action. Actions flow from personal sensations and then from intellectual reasoning, all organized around the individual with objectives that go beyond mere egoism to include family and social dimensions.


### Self-interest as a driving force for progress


Self-interest is a driving force behind process improvement and the quest for progress. To enrich oneself and bring satisfaction to oneself and others, one must act in a useful way, which naturally leads to innovation. Innovation and progress respond to humanity's changing circumstances, as new responses to new challenges are constantly required. Each improvement modifies the economic environment in which man lives, requiring new combinations to be found. Companies cannot be fixed, they must constantly innovate.


Yves Guyot, one of the last great figures of French liberalism at the end of the nineteenth century, dedicated his first book to the role of the inventor. It follows the logic of the facts of liberalism, driven by a taste for the satisfactions of body and mind. The individual naturally seeks his satisfactions, and to obtain them, he must organize his activity differently, finding new combinations and responding to hazards. Society as a whole observes that circumstances are changing, and that production conditions are evolving, allowing access to additional wealth by responding to them in an enlightened way. This recognition that improvements are possible is in line with human perfectibility, the conviction that the golden age lies not in the past but in the future.


### The State and its structural inability to innovate


The state does not innovate, and this fact is of considerable importance. Ancient societies such as Egypt, where professions were handed down from father to son, illustrate this phenomenon of fixed societies. The State reproduces this non-innovation by its very nature of functionarism. It relies on the activities of civil servants, who have no personal initiative or remuneration commensurate with their action or innovation. As everything is laid down in regulations, there is neither the will nor the interest to innovate, and the system itself is not designed for innovation. National education is a perfect illustration of this principle: it is not designed to improve knowledge, but simply to distribute knowledge, conceived as an asset to be spread around.


Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, writing in 1883, anticipated what collectivism would be like and predicted how communist experiments in Russia and elsewhere would actually work. He explains that these systems cannot wait for internal innovations, and must content themselves with copying those from outside. In semi-communist societies, innovation must come from the free sectors, as it is impossible to innovate in the monopolized sectors of public services. Rewarding progress proves impossible in the collectivist system, as there is no positive appreciation of innovation and therefore no choice to mobilize resources for progress.


### Rewarding progress in free societies


In systems of freedom, progress is rewarded in various ways. Industrial and commercial profits are the main form: when an innovative product reaches the market, the innovator, being the first to propose a new solution that satisfies consumer needs better and more cheaply, enjoys a kind of temporary rent. In modern societies, inventors are also associated with glory, unlike in ancient societies where they were frowned upon. This glory constitutes a form of remuneration, comparable to that received by great artists, poets or even politicians, whose material remuneration often remains low but is supplemented by prestige. Patents and intellectual property also enable progress to be remunerated through self-interest.


In a free society, self-interest leads to innovation and progress. In a system of unfreedom, self-interest is dammed and shattered in favor of a pre-defined social interest. Innovation and progress are viewed critically, as they were in ancient societies, and it's thought sufficient to do what you know how to do. Communist societies don't allow individuals of genius, with different ideas that nobody initially believes in, to bring their projects to fruition. The march of innovation certainly involves many failures, but also many successes which, in a system of freedom based on contracts and voluntary exchange, lead to that perfectibility spoken of by eighteenth-century liberals.



## Harmony of interests and peace

<chapterId>370f4e98-021b-5c81-a109-3b57b689905f</chapterId>




### Harmony of interests: a historical construct


The notion of harmony of interests is a fundamental pillar of liberal thought, although it is frequently dismissed with skepticism by its opponents. The latter often regard the idea as a utopian, unrealistic dream, based on the premise that human interests are fundamentally antagonistic. But this view is based on an erroneous assessment of reality and an incomplete reading of history. To understand this harmony, we first need to examine the circumstances in which human interests are actually in opposition.


In the first ages of mankind, human beings found themselves in direct competition for limited resources that nobody had produced. Nature offered fruit trees, fish in rivers and lakes, resources on the seashore, but no human society had created these riches. In this context, appropriation was by force and destruction rather than by production, leading to violent competition as each sought to take the other's share. In those days, there was only one way to get rich: take what your neighbor had, or compete for access to natural resources.


### The birth of peaceful exchange and production


The modern idea is based on a radically different principle: the production of new utilities. From the moment agriculture appears, when the wheat field becomes a source of wealth, truly voluntary exchanges are born, enriching both parties. This new, non-destructive production of goods profoundly transformed human relations. Relationships based on contract, respect for the rights of others and voluntary exchange now form a chain of harmony in the world and in societies, as the Abbé de Saint-Pierre, for example, emphasized in the French liberal tradition.


This harmony has been built up gradually, thanks to a number of converging factors. The opening up of the world, transport technologies enabling more regular and extensive exchanges, and security guaranteeing the smooth running of commercial relations have enabled the emergence of this chain of harmony between suppliers, customers and all economic agents. This interdependence becomes particularly apparent in times of crisis: a natural disaster or armed conflict on the other side of the world immediately leads to impoverishment for all participants in the exchange. The American Civil War of the nineteenth century is a perfect illustration of this phenomenon, which brought ruin to many textile industries in Europe, notably in Lyon, simply by severing trade relations.


### International peace as a condition for prosperity


The need for international peace flows logically from this global harmony. Since trade is a positive-sum game in which all parties mutually enrich each other, it becomes imperative to maintain security both within societies and between nations. Any disruption to the global economic order causes a nuisance that reverberates throughout the exchange system. Accidents, crises, climatic hazards and above all wars, by upsetting the order of production in the countries affected, impoverish the entire world population. Gustave de Molinari, in his late nineteenth-century work, calls for a war on war itself.


Admittedly, the effects of conflict on non-belligerent societies were much weaker in the past. In the fifteenth or sixteenth century, armed conflict in Africa or Asia would hardly have affected European populations, as the sphere of exchange remained restricted and societies lived in relative self-sufficiency. Today, increasing interdependence makes war increasingly harmful for everyone, which paradoxically constitutes a powerful argument in favor of peace.


### Towards collective learning for peace


The transition to a peaceful society is a long learning process. Human societies are still marked by ancient concepts of production through violence, conquest, hunting and gathering. New concepts of peaceful production, exchange and harmony must now be learned and disseminated. French liberal authors propose several resources to underpin this peaceful dimension of liberalism: the mobilization of public opinion to teach world harmony and mutually beneficial relations, the establishment of institutions guaranteeing the rule of law on an international scale, and the promotion of free trade to make this chain of harmony more perceptible. Liberalism thus presents itself as that great harmonious chain of exchanges and contracts, founded on freedom and property, enabling progress and improving the situation of every individual.



## The different varieties of unfreedom

<chapterId>747ec342-bccd-5203-a198-c6e75505801b</chapterId>




### The different forms of unfreedom in history


Having examined the foundations of freedom in the facts of human existence, and traced the historical evolution of freedom and constraint, we must now analyze systems of unfreedom to understand their failings, and why modern societies must move towards freedom-based solutions. Systems of unfreedom have taken a wide variety of forms throughout history, but they all share a common characteristic: they are based on extremely backward-looking notions, inherited from the oldest human societies. Primitive communism, which organized the sharing of land and tools within small spheres, was a response to the production and security circumstances of the time, but it already represented a fundamental limitation of the individual.


The extreme form of unfreedom manifests itself in the total subjugation of the individual and the destruction of his or her property. In the communist system, all property can be regulated and controlled by others, constituting a form of collective slavery. The individual retains only those few properties of self that cannot be broken, notably inviolable consciousness and the freedom to think metaphysically, since unmanifested thought remains under his control. However, all other freedoms disappear: the freedom to have a family, material ownership of things, ownership of land and ownership of one's own labor are replaced by the rule of a large, dirigiste, collectivist state. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, in his major work on collectivism published in 1883, brilliantly analyzes these different socialist systems and prophetically explains how communist systems actually work, questioning the vagueness that then surrounded the concrete description of communist society.


### Socialism as a moderate form of unfreedom


Socialism and interventionism represent a moderate form of unfreedom, where more and more private services are replaced by public ones, and individuals are held in trusteeship by a large regulatory state. Unlike communism, which attempts to organize all production and consumption in a dirigiste manner, socialism operates in a half-communist way. It is precisely this reduced, practical ambition that explains its persistence today, whereas communism failed because it represented too abrupt a return to the societies of the past. The modern individual had acquired, through intellectual work and the practice of freedom, the capacity and will to govern himself, something that communism destroyed entirely.


What socialism fundamentally shares with communism is the replacement of contract, free choice and individual autonomy by political direction. Voluntary, mutually beneficial exchanges give way to regulations and so-called public services. Political choice, necessarily collective and expressed through democratic elections, replaces individual choices that everyone can control directly. In a democracy, the citizen must acquiesce to an overall program among reduced options, with no real control over what happens next, unlike the down-to-earth decisions of everyday life.


### Discrete forms of unfreedom: taxes and regulations


A more insidious form of unfreedom persists in the form of confiscatory taxes and intrusive regulations. We're not talking here about the taxes used to finance the justice and police services that guarantee freedom and security, but those that operate a real confiscation. Intrusive regulations make choices on behalf of individuals, and delimit the boundaries of contracts and exchanges according to the ideas of those with political power. Gustave de Molinari points out, with his usual verve, that in many of these regulations, the owner is no longer truly the owner: he has ceded his property to the state and is now only a usufructuary or possessor of temporary enjoyment.


The example of the homeowner who wishes to repaint his house is a perfect illustration of this situation. The homeowner believes he has the choice to use and abuse his property without infringing on the rights of others, but he discovers that he is really only the usufructuary of a house belonging to the community. Inheritance and hindered free trade also reveal this dispossession: the impediments of regulations, laws and customs stand between the individual's voluntary, non-intrusive choice and the decisions imposed by public power.


### Freedom, property and equality: inseparable concepts


Freedom cannot be dissociated from property or equality, for these notions are deeply intertwined. Frédéric Bastiat asserted that freedom is property: to be free means to make whatever use one wishes of one's property, of oneself and of the things one legitimately possesses. The person whose property is truly respected is truly free, since he can dispose of himself, his forces and faculties for work, what he owns for commerce, and his intellectual faculties for free thought and expression.


Systems of unfreedom are characterized precisely by inequality: some individuals freely enjoy their property, while others are dispossessed and have their choices dictated by others. Yves Guyot fought all these battles at the end of the 19th century to defend feminism, combat racism and anti-Semitism, and oppose what is now known as national preference. A system which excludes part of the human race from the enjoyment of its own faculties is not a system of freedom, but of tutelage with precise geometry. Freedom of work, exchange and contract must apply to all without distinction, because preventing advantageous contracts by excluding one part of humanity contradicts the very principle of individual freedom that enables harmony in the world.


## Replacing contract with policy

<chapterId>061e5c30-cb50-5205-8a1b-e41b7d1d234a</chapterId>




### Criticism of the state as a divine entity


In the 19th century, particularly under the influence of German writers, a conception of the state developed that presented it as a kind of earthly deity. This fantasized vision attributed quasi-supernatural qualities to the state, describing it as an intangible, superior being, devoid of human defects, capable of directing society with exceptional foresight. The proponents of this conception readily compared the State to the brain of the social body, conferring on it a capacity for foresight and direction that no individual could possibly possess.


Paul-Laurent Beaulieu, who studied in Germany and knows this literature inside out, contrasts this idealized vision with a much more down-to-earth analysis of what the modern state is really like. The contemporary democratic state operates in a radically different way to the Ancien Régime, where power was derived from a family line and the hereditary position of royalty. The reality of the democratic state reveals that it is by no means a question of an omniscient god, but rather of men organized into parties vying for possession of political power. These parties organize a monopoly that is particularly harmful because it is temporary, leading to hasty enjoyment of the benefits of power, a waste of public resources, and laws drawn up by individuals who are not always suited to these functions.


### The electoral process and its intrinsic limitations


The electoral process differs fundamentally from the process of individual judgment. When an individual makes a personal decision, whether to enter into a contract, make an exchange or undertake a job, he or she makes observations and thinks carefully before acting. This deliberation takes place slowly and peacefully in everyone's mind. In the political sphere, on the other hand, decisions emerge from agitation and election fever, in a climate of short-termism that is hardly conducive to the enlightened judgment required by the complex issues that the state claims to deal with.


The democratic state also has the major drawback of systematically sacrificing minority interests. Since the decision-making process is based on the majority, the latter naturally tends, in the absence of sufficient constitutional constraints, to infringe on the individual rights of minority citizens. Worse still, future generations find themselves taxed in advance, inheriting debts and interest on expenditure to which they have never consented. This contradicts the great democratic principle of the American Revolution, that there can be no taxation without representation.


### The distinction between necessary and parasitic state functions


A careful distinction must be made between the legitimate functions of the state and its parasitic functions. Laws that guarantee the protection of individuals through a judicial and police system establish the security necessary for exchanges and contracts. These laws enable individuals to weave mutually beneficial contractual relationships with one another, and thus build society. From this point of view, laws that guarantee property, liberty and security must be seen positively as negative attributions of the state, i.e. attributions that merely protect without intervening.


Unfortunately, far too many laws hinder, prevent and regulate the activities of individuals without their infringing the rights of others. These laws are positive attributions of the state, replacing contract and exchange by majority decision and administrative regulation. Individuals are thus hindered in their ownership of things, or in their ownership of themselves, by pretexts of indefinite and indefinable harm to society or to certain categories of people.


### The superiority of contracts over political decisions


Contract-based human relationships enable a process of discovery and authentic deliberation. The individual, responsible for his fate and master of his faculties, decides to satisfy his needs and seeks solutions to improve his existence. This inner process is part and parcel of his own individuality, and is part of a process of free competition that constantly improves the alternatives on offer. The individual observes his own reality, feels the effects of his choices through pain or pleasure, and can freely modify his course of action. The contracts he forms are mutually advantageous for all parties.


Conversely, relationships based on political power function in a truncated way. Electoral agitation and misleading promises replace calm deliberation. Choices are too complex and all-encompassing for individuals to be able to measure their effects on themselves. On the market, everyone can choose freely between different alternatives and even create their own solution, whereas the market for political ideas remains restricted and under tutelage. Political choice is imposed by the majority and by package, forcing the individual to enter into disadvantageous exchanges, whereas contractual freedom would enable him to make of his faculties and properties the use he deems best to obtain satisfaction according to his own criteria.



## Power through taxation and monetary manipulation

<chapterId>ca113581-6969-58b4-a9f8-996ab409be25</chapterId>




### Taxes as an instrument of political domination


The fundamental distinction between contribution and taxation is the starting point for any reflection on unfreedom practices. Contribution represents a legitimate exchange in which the citizen participates in the financing of essential collective services, such as courts and police forces, which guarantee the security of transactions and enable peaceful social life. This contribution is akin to a collective purchase, a service rendered for a service paid for, in a logic of voluntary exchange.


Arbitrary taxation, on the other hand, is rooted in a radically different conception of the relationship between the individual and power. As early as the end of the 17th century, thinkers such as Pierre de Boisguilbert vigorously criticized the tax concepts of the Ancien Régime, where the sovereign considered himself the owner of all lands and subjects. Arbitrary pruning is a perfect illustration of this logic of political guardianship, in which the individual belongs to the nation and owes his or her resources at the whim of those in power. The French Revolution broke down some of these conceptions, but did not really replace them, as they remained so deeply rooted in people's mentalities.


The question of fair taxation also occupied liberal economists of the time. In their view, a legitimate tax should be proportional rather than progressive, as each individual protected by the common legal framework should contribute equally to society's burdens. Progressive taxation represents a return to ancient conceptions of the individual as the property of the group. Frédéric Bastiat developed this idea of an exchange of service for service, insisting on the need for taxes to be legible, so that taxpayers know precisely what they are getting in return for their contribution.


### Deficit and debt as intergenerational transfers


Alexis de Tocqueville identified the democratic problem of public spending particularly well. Political power, however well-intentioned, fails to be thrifty. This observation, formulated after his trip to the United States, anticipated the future functioning of democratic societies, where reducing public spending and state powers would prove extremely difficult.


The fundamental mechanism lies in breaking the link between enjoyment and punishment. Individuals have a natural tendency to shift burdens onto others, while enjoying the benefits themselves. Deficits and debt make it legally, if immorally, possible to pass on the cost of services obtained to future generations. This practice constitutes a major contradiction with the democratic principle that there should be no taxation without representation. Future citizens, who do not yet exist and do not take part in current deliberations, find themselves unable to defend their interests in the face of decisions that commit them. Democracy, which already tends to sacrifice minorities, also sacrifices future generations.


### Money as a tool of domination


In the logic of political domination, money ceases to be a commodity or service like any other, and becomes an instrument of power. Jurists and economists allied to this conception present money as a sign of sovereignty, asserting that a nation cannot exist without minting its own money. Yet the primary mission of the law should be to protect and organize the possibility of exchanges and contracts, not to take the place of society's participants by imposing this or that service on them.


As early as the Middle Ages, Nicolas Oresme analyzed the harmful effects of monetary manipulation on trade. Monetary decreases produce [inflation](https://planb.academy/resources/glossary/inflation) and destabilize prices, as the monetary signal becomes uncertain when the master of value measures can arbitrarily alter their content. Metallic money itself was often no more than an illusion, with coins containing low-value alloys that some 19th-century liberal writers referred to as copper assignats.


In the face of this domination, freedom offers solutions through competition and innovation. Bills of exchange and banknotes invented in the Middle Ages, and today private currencies, provide contracting parties with a means of exchange that is voluntarily accepted, rather than a currency with no intrinsic value used primarily to finance political power.


### The war and the denial of secession


Gustave de Molinari paid particular attention to the question of war in the 19th century, observing the contradiction between this archaic mode of international relations and the new phenomena of peaceful production and globalization. War is essentially a manifestation of the conflicting interests of rulers themselves, who seek to extend their monopoly of territorial domination as an industrialist would seek to increase his clientele.


This logic of expansion is a vestige of the feudal system, where the State was conceived as the owner of the nation. The history of territorial transfers between ruling families, by marriage or sale, illustrates this conception. The purchase of Louisiana or Alaska testifies to the possibility of acquiring not only land, but also the right to dominate its inhabitants.


The doctrine of liberty opposes this logic with the principle of individual and collective secession, expressing the free choice of individuals who belong to themselves. Yet even in America, considered a model of liberty, secession was denied to states wishing to recover their sovereignty. Modern constitutions proclaim a single, indivisible republic, prohibiting all collective emancipation. A region cannot join another country, nor can it become autonomous to manage its own public services. This fundamental contradiction in systems of unfreedom in no way prevents states from seeking to win over new political clienteles, thus perpetuating the very practices of domination they claim to proscribe.



## The different variants of protectionism

<chapterId>3c1cf3d1-e2f5-5a95-9671-bf82b4de4a4e</chapterId>




### Protectionism as a response to natural competition


Protectionism is one of the most significant manifestations of the unfreedom systems that replace the harmonious functioning of contracts and exchanges freely entered into. To understand this notion, we need to remember that competition is naturally imposed on human beings by the finiteness of available resources. This scarcity creates a need for acquisition and distribution, which can be achieved either by contract or by politics. Free competition distributes resources fairly, since it rewards the productive efforts of individuals. However, it inevitably leaves behind some less productive individuals who fail to make the right decisions in the economic and social process.


Protectionism then appears as a response to this competition of methods, ideas and products. It offers a solution to those who do not wish to keep pace with progress, and who feel overshadowed by the more enterprising individuals. These individuals seek not only to protect themselves, but also to extract wealth from the enterprising population to live at their expense. Democracy, by giving power to the masses, naturally tends to create this protectionism, as the masses are generally not very propulsive and do not appreciate progress. This tendency is often accompanied by hostility towards the rich, fuelled by envy and the belief that they don't deserve their wealth.


### Customs protectionism and the pillow of protection


Protectionism in international trade is the most common form of this notion. Yet freedom encourages free trade, since the ownership of things acquired through work legitimately confers the right to exchange them for other products and services. The liberal system is based on total free trade, which allows everyone to use their property as they see fit through mutually beneficial exchanges. These exchanges enrich society and create happiness by satisfying needs, while at the same time developing work as it becomes truly remunerative.


Conversely, protectionism favors the less propulsive, creating what 19th-century authors such as Harriet Martineau, Frédéric Bastiat, Gustave de Molinari and Paul Leroy-Beaulieu called "the pillow of customs protection". The less enterprising simply rest on their laurels, since competition from foreign entrepreneurs is not allowed. When producers in a foreign country discover innovative ways to produce more efficiently or better products, domestic producers can rest easy on this protective pillow. Customs impede the entry of competing goods by taxing them heavily, preventing domestic consumers from acquiring better products and creating a form of economic domination by domestic producers.


### Competition customs and the preventive system


The protectionist idea also applies to what was known in the 19th century as the customs of competitive examinations. These limitations on the freedom to work are designed to protect certain individuals by making it easier to memorize a program in a state-designed school than to let entrepreneurs advance in their profession in innovative ways. This right of entry severely limits competition through the creation of regulated apprenticeship schemes, to the apparent benefit of certain professions. However, this benefit remains illusory, as these professions are not as innovative as they could be. The routine customs of competitive examinations train individuals in a fixed way of working, preventing the emergence of geniuses, innovators and inventors who would take these professions to the next level.


This approach substitutes the preventive system for the repressive system, the only one accepted by liberalism. The repressive system is based on individual responsibility, where any infringement of the rights of others must be remedied. The preventive system, on the other hand, organizes regulations in advance that are supposed to prevent such infringements, but itself begins with an infringement of rights by preventing the non-infringing use of freedom. Arthur Mangin and other radical French liberal writers, such as Charles Dunoyer and Charles Comte, led this fight for freedom of work and a repressive system for proven nuisances, in the continuity of Turgot's fight against guilds.


### Labor protectionism and anti-immigration laws


A modern application of protectionism concerns the fight against immigration, a phenomenon linked to democratic forces. In the 18th century, it was commonplace for foreigners to hold public office, like Minister Necker, or to serve in the army, like the Swiss Guards. This openness contrasted with the current return to protectionism in public office. The Société d'économie politique, which brought together the great French liberals of the 19th century, examined the anti-immigration laws emerging in Anglo-Saxon societies. These countries, having dominated the natives, were entrenching themselves and refusing competition from other populations wishing to participate through contract and exchange, notably the Chinese wishing to work in the mines of Australia or California.


These authors identified labor protectionism as the future dominant form of protectionism. While the protectionism of big business aims to protect itself from foreign competition on products, worker protectionism seeks to protect itself from competition from foreign workers. This notion of protectionism thus reveals different shades of unfreedom, perniciously organized in the exchange of property through customs protectionism, in the freedom to work through competition customs, and in the freedom to travel and work through anti-immigration laws.



## The eternal failure of unfreedom

<chapterId>e4b15871-df1d-5d98-a395-16e24e7258d5</chapterId>




### The impossibility of planism anticipated by the physiocrats


The failure of unfreedom has a long intellectual history, dating back to the physiocrats of the 18th century. These thinkers opened a fundamental debate on the possibility of a society in which the State, by organizing unfreedom, would take decisions in place of individuals to create a supposedly more harmonious economic and social functioning. This reflection crystallized around the great question of grain and wheat, the major food resource of the time, whose trade was debated throughout the century.


Physiocrats, including Louis-Paul Abeille, Turgot, the Marquis de Mirabeau and Dupont de Nemours, developed decisive arguments against state planning. They demonstrated that the state's attempt to organize food production, fix prices, prevent the transfer of resources between regions, or prohibit certain exchanges, could not work. Political power lacks what they call the master's eye, the ability to perceive economic realities that only individuals possess in their daily lives. Economic phenomena, particularly in the case of a commodity as important as wheat, unfold on scales too large and complex for a central authority to grasp properly.


The nature of demand, supply, resources employed and prices are variables far too complicated for a political power to evaluate and make the system work optimally. Through freedom, on the other hand, each economic agent responds with his own intuitions to the signals he perceives, notably prices and competition, leading him to adopt appropriate responses to events. This intellectual legacy of the physiocrats was passed on to the French liberals and all the liberal economists of the 19th and 20th centuries.


### Paul Leroy-Beaulieu's predictions on collectivism


The French liberal tradition extended this analysis into the 19th century, notably through the work of Paul Leroy-Beaulieu and his major work on collectivism. Leroy-Beaulieu set out to analyze in advance how a communist society would function, since the theorists of communism, led by Karl Marx, had been very sparing with details of how the society that would replace capitalism would actually function. Socialism, after an apparent failure in 1848, was coming back in force around the Commune and, with democracy and universal suffrage, was becoming increasingly threatening.


Leroy-Beaulieu identified communism as a form of slavery worse than traditional slavery itself. In the communist system, there is no nest egg, no possibility of redemption. It's an inescapable slavery in which the individual is deprived of freedom, with no path to liberation, to self-ownership, individual autonomy and the freedom to act according to one's own ideas. Political freedom itself would necessarily be suppressed, as democratic functioning does not allow for the sustainable management of production and distribution processes. A permanent tutelary power becomes indispensable, and communists cannot accept that this power be transferred to opponents of the system.


### The suppression of individual freedoms in the collectivist system


The principle of unfreedom in production and distribution has direct consequences for every dimension of individual existence. Freedom of the home disappears in a collectivist system because there is no freedom of work. A superior decision dictates to individuals which branches of production they will participate in and how they will work. Since you live close to your job and can't choose where you work, you can't choose where you live. The freedom to start a family or maintain friendships is itself problematic, as exchanges - which are also a form of friendship - are regulated and controlled by state authority.


Freedom of the press suffered the same fate, for two fundamental reasons. Firstly, political freedom was severely restricted, since abandoning the communist form of society was unthinkable, making criticism of the system impossible. Secondly, as the state owns the means of production, it becomes the sole printer and decides on the distribution of books and periodicals. Without ownership of the press or printing houses, freedom of expression is completely eliminated. Freedom of consumption also disappears, as the state decides what will be produced and in what quantities, even if it will never achieve its objectives due to the fundamental impossibility of properly forecasting and organizing production.


### Historical confirmation of liberal analyses


The twentieth century brought dazzling confirmation to the predictions of French liberals. The communist system, now a reality, demonstrated the accuracy of the analyses that anticipated both the impossibility of planism and the complete tyranny such a regime would engender. In a liberal system, contracts are freely established between demanders and producers, in a harmonious and peaceful relationship. The collectivist system, on the other hand, organizes complete tutelage of the individual and individual relationships, replacing free self-direction with regulatory and arbitrary decisions by a political power.


This intellectual and factual history of the failure of unfreedom is a fundamental lesson. It shows that the extension of the history of humanity leads us to reject ever more the communist idea and the idea of guardianship, in order to bring about freedom, autonomy, individual decision-making and voluntary, peaceful exchange between people.


# Sophisms of unfreedom

<partId>de66dc14-7dc0-52c5-aedf-5efe4820b9c1</partId>


## Intellectual sophistry: false definitions of freedom

<chapterId>9bd81fd4-20ed-5305-845a-d081811946ee</chapterId>




### Sophism as an obstacle to freedom


The fifth part of this course on freedom as a project for society tackles a fundamental question: why does unfreedom continue to dominate our societies, even though it is refuted by the facts and contradicts the historical movement towards greater freedom? The answer lies in the existence of tenacious sophisms that serve as the intellectual foundation for systems of unfreedom. The notion of sophism has its origins in the thinking of Frédéric Bastiat, author of the famous Sophismes économiques. Bastiat believed that the socialists and interventionists of his day were wrong, but with good intentions. As the popular expression in many languages reminds us, the road to hell is paved with good intentions. For Bastiat, liberalism's adversary is not the sophist himself, but the sophism as a reasoning or factual error that needs to be corrected by economic science.


### Authentic definitions of freedom in the French liberal tradition


The 19th-century French liberal tradition provides a solid conceptual framework for understanding what freedom really means. Yves Guyot defines human rights as consisting in the free use of one's will and faculties, which corresponds to self-ownership and its materialization within the limits of the rights of others. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu makes an essential clarification, emphasizing that freedom does not consist in absolute security or freedom from all risks, but in man's ability to determine his own actions. This definition clearly distinguishes freedom from the power to ensure that the result of our actions always corresponds to our desires. Charles Comte completes this vision by defining freedom as the state of a person who encounters in his fellows no obstacle to the regular development of his being or to the innocent exercise of his faculties. These definitions converge on a conception of freedom rooted in self-ownership and mutual respect for individual rights.


### Montesquieu's dangerous definition and its critics


Faced with these rigorous definitions, political thought also conceals erroneous conceptions of freedom, the most famous of which is that of Montesquieu: freedom is the right to do whatever the laws allow. This seemingly innocent definition turns out to be extremely dangerous, as it allows any infringement of an individual's innocent freedoms as long as it is authorized by law. Nineteenth-century liberal thinkers generally criticized Montesquieu on this point. Destutt de Tracy devoted an entire work, his Commentaire sur l'Esprit des lois, to correcting the erroneous notions of this great thinker. Contrary to Montesquieu's conception, Benjamin Constant developed a theory clearly distinguishing the sphere of innocent, free individual action from the sphere of power, the latter simply guaranteeing the exercise of rights without determining the wills or actions of individuals.


### Negative freedom and positive freedom: a fundamental opposition


A crucial distinction is between negative and positive freedom. Negative liberty is defined as the absence of obstacles: the individual encounters no hindrance in the expression of his will, as long as he does not directly harm others. This concept is deeply rooted in the notion of property, as it designates the freedom to make innocent use of one's own property, body, faculties and legitimately possessed goods. Positive freedom, on the other hand, is conceived as the ability to do and to obtain: according to this vision, the individual is free when he can obtain the things he wants. This concept breaks with notions of property and paves the way for socialist justifications of state intervention. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, in his book Le Collectivisme, demonstrates that socialism, even with its different notions of freedom, prevents people from doing things, without guaranteeing the promised results. Socialism thus fails doubly, delivering neither the freedom conceived by liberals nor the freedom supposedly brought about by its own theories. Understanding these fundamental distinctions enables us to grasp how initial conceptual errors in the definition of freedom then give rise to sophisms in the economic, social and international spheres.



## The democratic fallacy: guardianship of guardians

<chapterId>96869664-df6e-5f47-92ad-ac93c88e4f55</chapterId>




### The fundamental paradox of democratic guardianship


The fallacy of guardianship of guardians is one of the most remarkable contradictions of contemporary political systems. We live within structures that multiply regulations, hindrances and public directions, which substitute for individual acts that might naturally flow from free perception, free judgment and personal will. This public guardianship is based on a precise premise: that citizens are fundamentally incapable of behaving for themselves, and require general direction to prevent potentially harmful acts. Rather than repress genuine infringements of the rights of others, the system prefers to anticipate and supervise, in the belief that individuals left to their own devices will make choices that are inappropriate, uninformed or even contradictory to their own interests.


Yet this same system of unfreedom operates under a democratic form that postulates exactly the opposite. The democratic mechanism asserts that all citizens, equally, are perfectly capable of behaving collectively and making informed political choices between different options. They can compare programs, evaluate political personalities, judge the foreseeable effects of proposed measures and assess the merits of envisaged reforms. The contradiction then becomes glaring: the same individuals deemed incapable of deciding for themselves in their daily lives are simultaneously capable of deciding for the community as a whole.


### The simplicity of economic choice versus the complexity of political choice


This contradiction is all the more surprising given that choice on the daily economic market is infinitely simpler than political choice. In our consumption decisions, we compare the satisfactions we derive from each act on a daily basis, the decision-making process remains direct, and the results are immediately apparent. We can compare the consequences of our choices with each other, and adjust our behavior in the light of experience. The knowledge required remains within our reach, since we consume goods that we know and can evaluate directly.


The political market presents radically different characteristics, making the choice much more difficult. Decisions are spaced out over time, and the consequences remain collective and therefore difficult to examine for each individual. A political measure may well affect people who are not directly visible to the citizen, making it impossible to fully understand the implications of his or her vote. Elections are moments of collective fever, when choices are made about entire programs, often complex and difficult to grasp in their entirety. If we were to logically conclude that there is an inability to choose, it would be precisely in this political market that this inability should manifest itself most strongly.


### Democratic despotism according to Tocqueville


Alexis de Tocqueville famously identified, particularly in the second part of Democracy in America published in 1840, what he called democratic despotism. This concept refers to the tendency for freedoms to be curtailed by the democratic mass itself, when the state becomes the director of society. The danger lies in democracy's turning against its own purpose: whereas it should guarantee respect for individual rights and effectively organize the repression of infringements of human freedoms, it is itself transformed into an instrument of infringement of these very rights and freedoms.


Tocqueville was long misunderstood precisely because of his nuanced position. He recognized the inevitability of democratic rule, while remaining clear-sighted about its dangers, in particular its spendthrift and despotic forces, which he was already observing in the United States of his time. His analysis retains a particular resonance today for all democratic societies facing the same challenges and experiencing the same perils.


### The need to limit democracy


Faced with these dangers, liberal thought, notably that of Benjamin Constant, developed the idea that democracy must be limited by constitutional rules and enlightened public opinion. Democracy is a means to an end, not an end in itself. This means is certainly good and necessary, but it remains dangerous if left unchecked. Interventionist and socialist systems push this guardianship to the limit, granting guardians the most extensive power while drastically reducing individual freedom. Recognizing the obvious contradiction between the supposed inability to choose for oneself and the asserted ability to choose collectively for others is the first step towards a truly liberal society, founded on respect for individual autonomy.


## The economic fallacy: work as a goal, not a means

<chapterId>ffec06b3-1fea-51c8-9bdc-d36efcfd82df</chapterId>




### Frédéric Bastiat's economic fallacies


As part of our study of freedom as a social project, we now turn to the economic fallacies that form the intellectual foundation of unfreedom systems. These fallacies underpin both communism and the various forms of excessive regulation and oppressive taxation. Frédéric Bastiat, a 19th-century French economist, devoted most of his work - sadly cut short by his untimely death - to identifying and deconstructing these fallacies. His work remains particularly relevant today, as economic freedom remains widely contested in our contemporary societies, allowing systems of compression and authority to remain alive and well.


Bastiat understood that to truly establish freedom, it was first necessary to destroy competing systems based on faulty reasoning. While these fallacies are easily recognized by the discerning eye, the general population does not always identify them spontaneously. The role of the liberal economist is therefore to point out these factual errors and reasoning flaws in the analysis of societies, exchange and production. For Bastiat, protectionism and socialism represented backward steps towards outdated economic conceptions, which had to be combated by rigorous demonstration of their logical inconsistencies.


### The inversion between work as a goal and work as a means


One of the major economic fallacies identified by Bastiat in his study of protectionism concerns the fundamental inversion between labor as an end and labor as a means. Protectionists present labor as an end in itself, seeking to defend it and get people to work more. This perspective leads them to value work as an expenditure of strength and energy, without paying attention to the concrete results of this activity. They celebrate the cost to the individual of this expenditure of strength, rather than questioning what it achieves.


Liberals, and Bastiat in particular, adopt a radically different perspective, focusing on the effects of work rather than on work itself. The real goal is to get workers the maximum satisfaction in exchange for the same amount of work, not to maximize work while losing sight of its purpose. This inversion between ends and means is frequently found in the argument for unfreedom. It can be seen, for example, in the case of political liberty, which is often presented as an ultimate goal, when in fact it is a means to the satisfactions of civil liberty, economic freedom and freedom of action. As Tocqueville demonstrated in On Democracy in America, the democratic system has its strengths and weaknesses, but its value lies in what it achieves, namely certain guarantees for the freedom of all and equality in freedom.


### False logical links and fictitious opposition of interests


Another fallacy of particular concern to Bastiat is that of "as a result of that, so because of that" reasoning, which covers the causal inversions and false logical connections established by socialists and protectionists. The latter construct quick reasonings in which, on close examination, obvious errors can be detected. They postulate conclusions that do not logically follow from the preceding propositions, and often build their theoretical scaffolding on factually false premises or ill-defined concepts. Recourse to the opposition between general and particular interests is also a frequent pretext for undermining individual rights and freedoms, by presenting this general interest as a superior entity authorizing all violations of freedoms considered inferior.


In his unfinished work Les Harmonies économiques of 1850, Bastiat develops the fundamental idea that interests in a liberal society are harmonic, not antagonistic. When individuals trade and enter into voluntary contracts, both parties benefit from the relationship. The opposition of interests belongs to the conceptions of societies of the most distant past, where the conditions of production effectively created conflicts, as between two tribes over the same hunting ground. This archaic vision unfortunately persists in contemporary economic ideas, through the notion of the balance of trade or the idea that we shouldn't enrich our so-called enemies. However, in the relationship between customers and suppliers, the vocabulary of enemies and enrichment at the expense of others simply has no place.


### The persistence of sophisms and the need to eradicate them


Bastiat insisted on this point because, in his day, England was constantly portrayed as an ogre seeking to dominate and enrich itself on the spoils of France and other nations. This discourse was perpetuated in the 19th and 20th centuries, and we hear it again in the 21st century with the current resurgence of protectionism. Protectionism is one of the forms of unfreedom that is back in the spotlight today, precisely because the same economic fallacies continue to underpin it. Eradicating such fallacious reasoning requires rigorous economic education, and particularly the dissemination of liberal economic thought, which has identified many of these errors in the analysis of human relationships in the marketplace.


Economic fallacies, however, are only one category among many. The intellectual fallacy concerning the wrong definition of freedom, the political fallacy opposing the inability to govern oneself and the alleged ability to govern others, as well as the social and international fallacies we'll examine later, all deserve special attention. The whole purpose of this analysis is to understand how some people attempt to justify unfreedom, and why these foundations turn out to be unstable and easily attacked, since they are based not on genuine reasons, but simply on sophisms.



## The social fallacy: social classes and capitalist exploitation

<chapterId>a154cdd1-5cc7-56c0-839b-a898305cdf30</chapterId>




### Social classes in ancient and modern societies


To understand the fallacy of capitalist exploitation, we must first examine the historical reality of social classes. Unfree societies have traditionally functioned with a high degree of social stratification, a feature still found in contemporary communist societies. This stratification fundamentally opposes those who have the freedom to choose for themselves and others, and those who are deprived of it. Charles Comte, in his "Traité de législation" (Treatise on Legislation), which Frédéric Bastiat considered his favorite book, examines the different states of society to show how freedom and unfreedom have functioned throughout history.


In ancient societies such as Egypt, India, Greece and Rome, social classes remained relatively fixed. Certain groups of individuals enjoyed freedom, while others were deprived of it, with a high degree of social reproduction where trades were handed down from father to son. In the Middle Ages, relations between serfs and lords perpetuated this fixed stratification, which considerably limited individual freedom. Modern society, since the French Revolution and even before, is characterized by individual autonomy, equality before the law and equal access to all professions. It is precisely this development that distinguishes modernity from earlier societies.


### The futility of Marxist social categories


The socialist and communist interpretation asserts that social classes persist in modern societies, maintaining less freedom for certain parts of the population. However, when we rigorously analyze what social classes may be in the present, we discover fundamental contradictions. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, in his book "Le Collectivisme", attempts to take seriously the claims of socialists and communists by examining their vocabulary opposing workers and laborers to the elite possessing the means of production.


These notions are extremely complex to apply in practice. Wealthy artists and singers don't own the means of production. A great footballer would logically be a proletarian, since the ball does not belong to the players. Similarly, a journalist, a great scientist or a lawyer would be proletarians, since they work with their hands and their intellect, without owning the tools of their trade. In his "Essay on the Distribution of Wealth", Leroy-Beaulieu demonstrates that modern societies are organized around work, and that we must constantly redeem our wealth through work. The development of capital is itself a daily task of market analysis and investment. This reality reveals the futility of the social categories inherited from Marxist analysis in a society founded on equal rights and freedom to work.


### The real beneficiaries of capitalist development


Contrary to the idea of exploitation, certain traditionally disadvantaged groups benefit particularly from the development of the market economy. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, in "Le travail des femmes au XIXe siècle", shows that women benefit from capitalist progress. In ancient societies, their lot was less than enviable, due to their lesser productive power and the lack of security that further penalized them. In capitalist societies based on voluntary exchange, security has improved and contracts are based on free will. As work becomes increasingly mechanized and intellectual, women are able to earn better wages and enjoy a more enviable status.


The lot of children also improved thanks to this evolution. As work became more and more intellectual, it became impossible to get children to work immediately. Training through education, slow and costly, ensures a better lot and greater respect for them. With regard to environmental issues, liberal authors recognize the nuisances of dangerous and unhealthy industries, deforestation and pollution, while asserting that one cannot harm others freely, thus delimiting a perimeter of free action without nuisance.


### The real trend in free societies and liberal sensitivity to poverty


The socialist assertion that the poor become poorer and poorer in capitalist societies is contradicted by the facts. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu demonstrates that the real trend in societies based on contract and voluntary exchange is the constant improvement of the lot of the masses. Consumer goods become available at ever-lower prices, and labor productivity continually improves. This can easily be verified by observing the size of dwellings, the quality and frequent renewal of clothing, and the improvement of food.


Nevertheless, liberal writers have a strong sensitivity towards poverty and the weak, inherited from the Age of Enlightenment. Turgot's fight for freedom of labor was aimed precisely at enabling everyone to make a living from their work, including women excluded from male guilds. Bastiat, in the 19th century, led his fight for free trade explicitly for the poor, since free trade enables the worker to derive maximum satisfaction from his daily work, while protectionism makes all the commodities he needs more expensive. These thinkers proposed liberal solutions to poverty based on freedom, contract, human sympathy and association, the latter being an extension of the individual that gives new strength to individual will and action.


## The international fallacy: national property before private property

<chapterId>e4cc0069-d2b8-5c33-8dc3-ccc2655afe68</chapterId>





### The fundamental contradiction between national and private property


Socialist and communist thought take a particularly paradoxical theoretical stance on property. These currents of thought regularly attack private property, believing that individual appropriation of things and land is contrary to justice. According to this vision, we should live in a world where wealth is distributed in a common and equal manner, or according to a collective and political organization aimed at balancing the distribution of resources. This criticism naturally extends to inheritance, seen as an unfair transmission of wealth that should be politically organized rather than left to the free decision of individuals.


For liberals, on the other hand, inheritance is a logical extension of the right of ownership. If you are a true owner, you have the freedom to do with your wealth as you wish, whether to give it away, sell it or pass it on to your heirs. Free exchange itself rests on this fundamental respect for the ownership of things and the ownership of oneself. Passing on some or all of one's wealth to one's heirs in no way infringes on the equal rights of others, which makes this practice perfectly legitimate from a liberal perspective.


### The origins of national ownership and its parallel with individual ownership


To understand the paradox that exists among socialist thinkers between their rejection of private property and their defense of national property, we need to analyze how property is born within its collective and national boundaries. Ancestral tribes and human groups took possession of territories, sometimes by violence, but often simply by the first discovery of new lands. The task of discovery was by no means a simple one, and was accompanied by a considerable conservation effort in the face of the constant dangers posed by settling in a new place.


The transfer of a human group to an unfamiliar area was fraught with difficulties, since people had to survive in new conditions when they had previously known where to get their means of subsistence. They had to fight against the climate and the animals of prey, and this work of taking possession was often paid for at the cost of their lives. Over the generations, these human groups not only extracted their means of subsistence from this territory, they also improved it through farming, digging canals and building roads. This process resembles exactly that by which an individual improves his own property after taking possession of it and clearing it.


### The fallacy of variable-geometry recognition


The contradiction becomes clear when we observe that socialists and communists are perfectly willing to recognize the boundaries and borders of national groups, while rejecting private property. In the analysis made by the modern successors of these currents of thought concerning wars of conquest or attacks by one country against another, they pose as defenders of frontiers. But what is a frontier if not the recognition of the limits of a group's collective ownership of its territory? In this way, we recognize that a group of individuals deserves guaranteed respect for its property, including armed intervention to protect these borders.


This position becomes particularly awkward as we move down the community ladder. In micro-states, socialists also respect collective property, but how far should a society reduced to a single family have its property respected? The first level is, of course, the individual. If we respect the boundaries of property in a group, why not respect it at the individual level? This logic reveals that we are still prisoners of ancient tribal conceptions, where collective ownership of a hunting ground was exclusive to all other tribes, without being conceived as belonging to the whole human race.


### Implications for individual freedom


We can also turn the inheritance argument against socialists by asking them why they defend inheritance in its collective forms. National ownership of land, like that of France, implies that it is always French people who inherit from French people. So why shouldn't the world's underprivileged, those who live in deserts or unfavorable climates, also inherit? Why not redistribute wealth worldwide? Socialists won't admit it, because they fully recognize the ownership of borders at the national level, but refuse to recognize it at the individual level.


This analysis also enables us to understand how unfreedom systems undermine individual autonomy. Taxes, when they exceed the legitimate contribution of service for service, are a form of territorial aggression against individual property. Regulation, too, represents a form of dispossession, as when a homeowner is forbidden to paint his house the color he wishes. These interventions dispossess us of our own property, whether it's the property of ourselves, of things or of land. The international fallacy thus reveals a variable-geometry recognition of property, where states and nations benefit from protection of their collective property, while the individual who acquires goods through work and exchange must endure regulation, supervision and tutelage.


# Philosophy of liberalism

<partId>67ec4570-8d65-5d03-8ce3-12caa4a132ea</partId>


## Freedom of contract and employment

<chapterId>085d5756-4ffe-5e2e-8b41-9a94102c97d3</chapterId>




### Freedom of contract as the foundation of a free society


The contract is the fundamental element underpinning free societies, as it represents the concrete expression of the free disposition of oneself and of the goods that legitimately belong to us. This contractual dimension derives directly from the principle of self-ownership: because each individual is the owner of himself, he mobilizes this ownership in his contractual commitments, where his will is bound to that of others in order to accomplish certain actions. In the same way, properties derived from previous work and exchanges can be mobilized in these contractual relationships, which should be understood as a positive-sum game in which each party finds an advantage in participating.


Exchange and contract are mutually enriching, since each individual finds satisfaction in acting in this way rather than abstaining. The contract always represents one option among others, and when a person chooses to enter into a contract to exchange products or services, it is precisely because he or she finds it advantageous to carry out this transaction. The voluntary contract is also the cement of society itself, which can be defined as the multiplicity of exchanges between individuals. Without exchange or contract, society exists only in fact, but it takes real shape through contract and voluntary association. This contractual functioning is the foundation of social harmony and peace, since it is based on mutually advantageous and freely negotiated relationships.


### Freedom to work and its far-reaching implications


Work is one of the most fundamental of human acts, and its freedom encompasses a remarkably wide field of application. Many activities can be considered as work: freedom of expression, for example, is also a form of freedom of work, since journalism, the press and printing are professions. Religious tolerance itself touches on this freedom, since the celebration of mass represents work. Guaranteeing freedom of work is therefore tantamount to guaranteeing an extremely comprehensive freedom that encompasses a wide range of actions.


This freedom is justified by a number of inescapable facts. Human needs vary from one individual to another, as do the means of satisfying them, and work is precisely this means of creating satisfactions and products for exchange. There is a fundamental diversity between the strengths and aptitudes of different individuals, who evolve in a changing environment that demands this or that skill in order to obtain wealth. These individuals also pursue different ends at different stages of their lives, and may prioritize physical or mental effort depending on circumstances.


Freedom of labor makes competition work, and has a propulsive character that makes progress possible. Human perfectibility is achieved through this freedom, because centralized management simply reproduces production patterns that are already known. The freedom to work enables us to break free from the well-trodden paths and move towards new creations.


### Obstacles to a freedom program


A coherent program of freedom of work implies combating its opponents and the barriers that stand in its way, notably regulated professions, what we might call "competition customs", and public monopolies. These barriers are often established in the name of a precautionary principle, a preventive rather than repressive system, on the pretext that certain individuals might commit abuses in a given profession. A system of guardianship is then established over all those contributing to this branch of work.


Acknowledging freedom to work means recognizing the superiority of the repressive system, which punishes real abuses and effective violations of the rights of others, over the preventive system, which hinders everyone's activity a priori. This recognition naturally leads to the transformation of more public services into private ones, replacing guardianship with freedom. The result is a society increasingly, if not exclusively, based on contract and voluntary exchange.


### Monetary and banking freedom as an application of the liberal program


Banking and monetary freedom is a particular application of freedom of labor, since money simply represents a service rendered and a product exchanged on the market. Numerous French liberal economists, including Jean-Gustave Courcelle-Seneuil with his book La Banque libre, helped to revitalize the economic theory behind this freedom in the 19th century. These thinkers offered a comprehensive critique of early monetary practices of manipulation, devaluation and inflation, as the history of state practice of money proved particularly dark and easily criticized.


Courcelle-Seneuil simply asserted the French Revolution's ideas on freedom of labor and property, explaining that there was no reason to justify a public monopoly of money. Free banking would make banking and monetary services available to more people, including farmers who were excluded from the system at the time. As in Scotland and certain American states of the time, this freedom of banking and money encouraged innovation and progress, thanks to the self-interest that motivated market players to develop new solutions to satisfy consumers. This liberal economic program must be widely defended by returning to the fundamental principles of freedom and self-ownership, to counter the interventionism still commonly applied.



## Reduced state powers

<chapterId>abb3498a-0232-5a58-aea0-52730b75ee84</chapterId>




### The reduction of state powers in liberal thinking


The political program of liberty as a project for society is fundamentally based on a drastic reduction in the powers of the state, particularly those considered parasitic. This idea runs through the entire French liberal tradition, from the Marquis d'Argenson and the physiocrats in the 18th century, with their famous "laissez faire" principle, to the great thinkers of the 19th century such as Paul Leroy-Beaulieu. While these authors recognize that the State has a number of necessary powers, notably in the fields of policing, justice and certain land-use planning, they all agree on the need to strictly limit its scope of action.


The debate on the functions of the state has always been intense within the liberal family, but a consensus is emerging around a fundamental distinction. On the one hand, there are those attributions that are conceived as necessary, i.e. principally the establishment of a rule of law with rules of justice, the protection of freedom of contract and the enforcement of contracts, and the defense of individual rights against violation. At the other end of the spectrum are ancillary functions, many of which are perfectly suited to private initiative, free association and the market economy. Indeed, history shows that roads and bridges have often been built and managed privately in many societies.


### The structural inefficiency of state action


The state, in all its economic manifestations, is a deficient and costly mechanism. Its limited productive power is due to the fact that it does not rely on individual initiative and enterprise, and does not allow personal interest to develop fully. The functionarism that characterizes it generates an organization that is not very progressive, and poorly adapted to the rapid evolution of society. This maladaptation becomes particularly problematic in a context of constant technical and technological innovation, where progress must be constantly monitored, even anticipated, and the production of goods and services constantly improved.


A return to centralized, political management therefore makes no sense in modern societies. Propulsive competition and individual and collective freedom must gradually replace state intervention. It is important to emphasize that it is not the individual alone who replaces the state, but free association and the coordination of individual forces on the basis of exchange and contract. Individualism does not replace socialism or interventionism: it is free enterprise and voluntary cooperation that take over far more effectively.


### Decentralization: insufficient criticism


The program to reduce State powers must not be confused with simple decentralization. While the latter has some merit in recognizing the variety of human existence, the local state has no more legitimacy than the central state to intervene in ways that are abusive and contrary to the fundamental freedoms of individuals. Simply transferring powers from the central state to local authorities, town halls or regions is not a satisfactory solution. The real aim is to transfer these powers to the realm of private activity, to the individual, to free association and enterprise.


The multiplicity of fronts on which to wage this battle makes the task difficult but exciting. A large number of services are monopolized by the state, while almost all others are subject to fierce regulation, which places so-called free activities under guardianship.


### The revealing case of education and health


Education and healthcare are perfect examples of the perverse effects of state monopolization. Paul Leroy-Beaulieu, in his book on the distribution of wealth published in 1885, anticipated that the great doctors and elite professors would become the privileged people of the future in a society based on contract. He believed that, as life became increasingly precious to individuals in an advanced system of civilization, those who protected and enriched it would see their value recognized in due measure.


The current reality reveals a paradoxical and saddening situation. Education and healthcare professionals, who produce extremely valuable services, are in fact the first to be sacrificed by the public service system. This system devalues their work and prevents them from acquiring the place in society that should normally be theirs. The state considers these branches to be so essential that it must monopolize them, but it is precisely because they are so useful that they would be highly valued in a regime of freedom. The liberal political program thus aims to restore freedom of work by abolishing public monopolies, putting an end to excessive regulation and recognizing the principles of autonomy and contract, which must take the place of politics in human exchanges.




## The liberal alternative to the welfare state

<chapterId>8be24b04-53da-5d80-8e76-3b6f3530723f</chapterId>




### Liberal alternatives to the welfare state


The liberal perspective on social issues is fundamentally based on a particular conception of human existence, centered on individual freedom and personal responsibility. Human relations are naturally organized around the contract, but also around what we might call human sympathy and the individual's natural associations. These associations include the family circle, relatives and close friends, as well as the institutions in which each individual evolves, be they free associations or companies.


This social organization allows for the emergence of voluntary solidarity, into which everyone enters of their own free will, but which can nonetheless reach considerable proportions. The mutual aid societies that developed in the 19th century are a perfect illustration of this phenomenon. These organizations were in fact the ancestors of today's welfare state and social security systems, with the difference that they operated on the basis of competition rather than state monopoly. This competition between establishments and methods enabled a continuous improvement in practices, favoring the success of the most efficient forms and the abandonment of those that failed.


The power of free association deserves special recognition in this analysis. The isolated individual has always found himself in a weak position when faced with the vagaries of life and the evolution of his productive capacities over the course of his life. It is precisely for this reason that human beings have lived in society since the time of the tribes. Association and private enterprise must therefore be the solution to the state's declining powers, because they are the ones who can replace the state, not the individual alone. Private assistance, unlike regulatory and bureaucratic public assistance, enables us to elevate man by respecting his individuality and pushing him towards progress, whereas public assistance tends to degrade him and keep him in a guardianship comparable to that of a serf or slave.


### Feminism and liberalism


The liberal analysis of feminism is based on a historical and anthropological study of the fate of women in different societies. Charles Comte, in his Traité de législation, a work particularly appreciated by Frédéric Bastiat, examines the condition of different population categories in ancient societies and in those recently discovered in Australia and New Zealand. His analysis reveals that in these primitive societies, the lot of women was not very enviable, as they were truly the slaves of their time.


The emancipation of women was achieved through freedom and what we call capitalism, i.e. the use of tools and machines that gradually gave women a productive capacity equal to, or even greater than, that of men. This technological development led to a greater emphasis on perseverance and moral and intellectual virtues, whereas early societies were focused on brute force. The central notion of consent is the fundamental point of convergence between feminism and liberalism, since contract and free exchange are based entirely on this principle. The system of guardianship and constraint, on the other hand, is based on non-consent or majority consent, which dispenses with the individual, day-to-day agreement of each person.


### The liberal position on racism


The question of racism was the subject of numerous discussions at the Société d'économie politique in the 19th century, which brought together France's leading liberal intellectuals and regularly welcomed foreign thinkers. These debates addressed various manifestations of racism, from anti-Semitism and the fate of Jews in Romania to the situation of Chinese in California and Australia. The vast majority of the society's members took a positive view of the mixing of human groups, believing that competition is propulsive and that the work of generations, combining different elements of culture and intelligence, leads to the best possible development of civilization, provided this is done on the basis of contract and voluntary cooperation.


The individualistic character of the doctrines of liberty directly contradicts collective representations of races or nations as homogeneous groups. Liberalism considers the individual and cannot apprehend a race as a whole. It must be recognized, however, that liberalism cannot replace one racism with another by imposing involuntary human relationships. The human choice of whether or not to associate with a particular individual, for whatever reason, must be respected in all its forms.


### Immigration from a liberal perspective


The question of immigration must be approached from the liberal fundamentals of self-ownership and ownership of things. When immigration is based on voluntary exchange, legitimate appropriation of goods and equal contracts, freedom of immigration must be asserted. Gustave de Molinari, himself a Belgian immigrant who lived in France, epitomizes this position. A great traveler who traveled throughout Russia, the United States and South America, he was a resolute opponent of the immigration restrictions that were beginning to appear in the 19th century.


Molinari and the vast majority of French liberals of his time viewed immigration positively, respecting the contributions of freedom of work and movement. They recognized that the fight against immigration constituted a new form of protectionism, a labor protectionism comparable to that of the big bosses protecting their profits from international competition. Just as industrialists wished to reserve a national market for themselves, workers and their political representatives, under a system of universal suffrage, saw competition from foreign workers as an embarrassment. The social liberal program was therefore not limited to economic issues, but also encompassed the retreat of the state and politics in favor of contract and free trade, in a perspective perfectly compatible with contemporary feminist and anti-racist movements.



## Individual autonomy and collective self-defence

<chapterId>1811a240-b5c8-5c34-906a-a5a030ca847f</chapterId>




### The individual as the foundation of political freedom


The doctrine of freedom rests on a fundamental principle that deserves to be clearly established: the individual is the inescapable starting point for any reflection on freedom. This assertion is not merely theoretical, but derives from a factual observation of human nature. We don't enjoy things collectively, we don't have an innate collective will, but an individual will of our own. Certainly, through exchange and contract, collective decisions can emerge, but the primary foundation always remains the individual, not the nation, the group or any other collective category.


This recognition of the individual's freedom and autonomy has important consequences for political organization. Non-freedom almost always manifests itself in the subjugation of the individual in the name of a supposed collective interest. Laws must therefore be based on the recognition of human rights and fundamental freedoms, while framing this freedom so that it does not infringe on the similar freedoms of others. Benjamin Constant, in his Commentary on Filangieri's work as well as in his Principles of Politics, insists on the necessary silence of the law when the individual makes a just and legitimate use of his liberty and property. This silence of the law is an essential principle to be re-emphasized today, as the only permissible limitation on individual freedom lies in the protection of the freedoms of others.


### Nations and the contractual basis of society


The liberal society is based on voluntary exchange and contract, which profoundly transforms our understanding of the nation and all communities. Associations, businesses and exchanges of all kinds must be voluntary. Nations themselves are to be understood as large associations through and within which individuals organize the administration of justice and the police. This definition makes it clear that nations are not superior entities that dominate or possess individuals.


History teaches us that the pretexts usually invoked to found national unity, such as unity of language, culture or history, do not stand up to impartial analysis. The wars that have enlarged certain territories to the detriment of others have not been waged on the basis of linguistic or cultural affinities, but according to opportunities for political patronage on the part of the powers that be. The great movement of colonization is a perfect illustration of this expansion of political clientele, with no regard for the unity of language or culture. We therefore need to think differently about politics, gradually replacing political serfdom with increasingly contractual and voluntary bases.


### Secession as an expression of freedom of government


The notion of secession is the logical continuation of this reflection on political freedom. Gustave de Molinari explored this question in particular depth, especially at the time of the American Civil War. This long and costly war raised not only the question of slavery, but also that of political autonomy and what Molinari called freedom of government. The fundamental question is whether one can freely choose one's government, or whether one remains in trusteeship, possessed by a nation from which one cannot exclude oneself by one's own doing.


True freedom of contract does not simply consist in being party to a social contract, but implies the freedom to sign or not to sign, and thus to choose to enter or leave a group under voluntary conditions. Collective secession enables a group of people to break away from one nation to form another, or to join an entity that has already been formed. Territorial or geographical pretexts are no more valid than linguistic ones, as demonstrated by the existence of landlocked states and political groups sharing different languages.


### Towards international security based on law


In his work of 1713-1717, the Abbé de Saint-Pierre had already identified the need for organized international security. Historically, security was established gradually, first between tribes, then on a national scale with the end of serfdom and the creation of vast, powerful states. Today, the proportion of human relations on an international basis has grown considerably, making it ever more imperative to have security on very broad boundaries.


Such a state of international law, which would recognize freedom of action while punishing infringements of that freedom, does not yet exist on a global scale. We therefore need to think about it and prepare for its organization, as history is moving towards an increasingly liberal functioning of societies. Security is an indispensable condition for voluntary human relations, since without it, innocent freedom can neither be recognized nor practically implemented. Freedom that is constantly in danger is freedom that is destroyed in advance, reduced to the freedom to think for oneself.



## Freedom rooted in human facts

<chapterId>fa1ae25f-bdcf-50db-9a12-d880ff568875</chapterId>




### The foundations of freedom in human facts


This course on freedom as a project for society is based on a fundamental conviction that deserves to be forcefully reiterated: freedom is neither a utopia nor a mere abstract theory, but is deeply rooted in the facts of human existence. The first part of the course established this essential foundation by examining the realities that characterize the human condition in its relationship to the world. Among these fundamental facts, self-ownership occupies a central place, for it is precisely because man owns himself that he can legitimately possess things, material goods and land. This human variety, this irreducible individuality, is the foundation on which any serious reflection on freedom is built. The competition of things in the world is also one of those inescapable facts that underpin the necessity of freedom as a mode of social organization.


An examination of the historical conditions of freedom reveals that it is a gradual and slow process. Ancient societies lived in very specific conditions, marked by extreme hazards and constant danger. These small communities operated according to modes of production based on violence and the collective appropriation of wealth, rather than on the creation of new value. However, history shows a remarkable tendency for authority to be replaced by contract, which makes it clear that socialist and interventionist solutions are in fact backward-looking. Communism appears to be the most regressive form of this trend, taking us back to the time of tribes on a national and international scale.


### The concrete workings of freedom and its mechanisms


The third part of the course explored the concrete mechanisms by which freedom produces its beneficial effects. Prices and profits play an essential role in this process, enabling free exchange to achieve a satisfactory outcome for all participants. Exchange and contract form the basis of this system, ensuring that every transaction is mutually advantageous for all concerned. The satisfaction of needs and the material and intellectual enjoyment to which every individual aspires find their natural fulfillment in economic freedom.


In contrast, the study of unfreedom in its various historical and theoretical forms shows that it is not equally rooted in fact. From complete communism to more moderate forms such as excessive taxation or over-regulation, unfreedom breaks the fundamental human bonds of individuality and will. It substitutes guardianship for personal choice, and replaces the contract with politics, with its defective and costly functioning. Authors such as Paul Leroy-Beaulieu in the nineteenth century, and even the physiocrats, had perfectly anticipated this failure, particularly during the great debate on free trade in grain that shook French and European society from the second half of the eighteenth century onwards.


### The fallacies of unfreedom and their refutation


To maintain itself despite its repeated failures, unfreedom must take on theoretically erroneous forms that can be described as sophisms. These distorted representations of reality enable the public to retain confidence in systems which, if properly analyzed, collapse as soon as they are seriously examined. The international fallacy, which establishes an artificial distinction between national and private property, is a perfect illustration of this tendency. Other economic, political and democratic sophisms, such as that of guardianship of guardians, reveal aberrations of the mind that support a system with no real foundation and perfectly predictable failures.


### A program of freedom through reform and public opinion


The program of freedom that emerges from this analysis must be bold, for it is founded on the real principles of human nature: self-ownership, individual autonomy and contract. This program implies a reduction in the powers of the state and a transformation of public services into private ones, with increasing intervention by free exchange. However, the French liberal tradition teaches that reforms are to be preferred to revolutions. Tocqueville masterfully demonstrated in L'Ancien Régime et la Révolution that revolutionary upheavals cost a lot without transforming society as much as they would like, often resulting in the consolidation of centralizing power.


The example of agitation for the abolition of the Corn Laws in nineteenth-century England illustrates the way forward. Richard Cobden and the Anti-Corn Law League, celebrated by Frédéric Bastiat, achieved success by mobilizing public opinion through meetings held throughout England, Scotland and Ireland. This method of convincing people through the debate of ideas remains the goal to be pursued today, as it is perfectly in tune with the values of competition, freedom and perfectibility that liberalism stands for.
